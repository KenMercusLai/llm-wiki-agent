# Disciplinary Self-Protection After Harm

Disciplinary self-protection after harm is the pattern where a profession responds to injury or scandal by protecting its access, reputation, methods, and senior figures before it fully accounts for the victim. [[113-henglieta-yu-nachang-jiang-renleixue-songshang-shenpanxi-de-moushaan-756562827]] introduces it through the anthropology response to [[HenriettaSchmerler|Henrietta Schmerler]]'s murder.

The episode's claim is not that [[RuthBenedict]], [[MargaretMead]], or [[FranzBoas]] lacked all ethical concern. Its criticism is that fieldwork access, anti-racist politics, scholarly reputation, and institutional survival could all pull attention away from Schmerler's truth and toward managing what the case would mean for anthropology.

## Key Claims
- Academic disciplines can define a case as a threat to the field before they define it as harm to a person.
- Protecting access to research communities can become morally evasive when it requires softening facts about violence.
- Women's entry into fieldwork can be restricted after a case, even when the real lesson should be better protection and accountability.
- Senior scholars' caution may be understandable under political pressure, but it still changes whose story gets preserved.
- A responsible discipline needs memory practices that do not turn victims into examples of what not to do.
