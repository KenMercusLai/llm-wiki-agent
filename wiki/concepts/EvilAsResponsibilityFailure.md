# Evil As Responsibility Failure

Evil as responsibility failure is [[39-zhexuejia-yu-lang-zai-langgeduoke-yongheng-de-xiatian-611233065]]'s ethical frame for acts that harm the helpless while hiding behind ordinary motives, institutions, fear, or non-reflection. The episode develops it through shock experiments on dogs, a child-abuse case, and [[MarkRowlands|Mark Rowlands / 马克·罗兰兹]]'s claim that evil should not be excused just because the actor does not experience themselves as malicious.

The frame has two parts. A person can fail the moral responsibility to protect a vulnerable being, and can also fail the knowledge responsibility to examine whether their beliefs, excuses, or institutional role are justified. That makes evil less a theatrical hatred of goodness than a refusal to see and answer for what one is doing.

[[103-ta-shi-cichuan-yewu-de-guang-ta-shi-hanna-alunte-737939789]] extends the concept through [[BanalityOfEvil]]. The episode's [[AdolfEichmann|Adolf Eichmann]] reading shows a large-scale political version of the same failure: evil can proceed through office language, legality, and career normality when people refuse to examine what their role makes possible.

## Key Claims
- Evil cannot be reduced to abnormal psychology, social explanation, or confessed bad intention.
- Harming a helpless being is ethically serious even when framed as research, discipline, fear, obedience, or "for their own good."
- Not reflecting is itself part of responsibility failure when reflection is available and the stakes are another being's suffering.
- The moral test is especially sharp where one party has power and the other cannot explain, refuse, or escape.
- Bureaucratic non-reflection can be a responsibility failure even when the actor describes the work as ordinary duty.
