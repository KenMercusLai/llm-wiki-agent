# All-Business-Class Supersonic Model

The all-business-class supersonic model is [[BlakeScholl]]'s early [[BoomSupersonic]] product-market insight in [[tsr-s5-blakescholl-v3-finalaudio-tsr-s5-blakescholl-v3-finalaudio]]. Instead of starting with a sonic-boom breakthrough or a large mass-market jet, Scholl targeted passengers already paying high fares for international business or first class.

The model treats speed as a substitute for cabin space. Scholl argues that if a flight is much shorter, premium passengers need less lie-flat sleeping space, so the same cabin area can hold more seats while still delivering a better trip. That changes the economic question from "can supersonic flight be a luxury prestige object?" to "can speed make premium seat density and operating cost work?"

In the source, this framing distinguishes Boom from Concorde and from NASA's two-option framing of future supersonic travel as either a private jet needing a sonic-boom solution or a large jumbo jet with hard economics. Boom's initial model could focus on overwater routes and defer [[BoomlessCruise]] until engine and flight-profile work made it more plausible.

## Key Claims
- A narrower premium segment can be a more plausible first market for capital-intensive hard tech than a broad consumer market.
- Product economics can change when one attribute, such as speed, substitutes for another expensive attribute, such as cabin sleeping space.
- The model does not remove certification, manufacturing, fuel-burn, or airline-adoption risk; it narrows the first business case enough to start.
