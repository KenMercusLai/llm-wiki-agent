# Rare Earth Processing Bottleneck

Rare earth processing bottleneck is the supply-chain problem emphasized in [[how-to-get-what-greenland-has-with-permission]]. The episode argues that mineral security is not just a question of owning deposits: rare earths must be mined, separated, refined, and turned into usable inputs for weapons, vehicles, electronics, and industrial production.

The source says [[Greenland]] has attractive heavy rare earth deposits, but that those deposits sit under extreme Arctic conditions and would take decades and major infrastructure to develop. [[GracelandBaskaran]] therefore treats Greenland as strategically relevant but not as a near-term fix for the [[UnitedStates]].

The bottleneck is geopolitical because the episode says [[China]] processes roughly 90 percent of the world's rare earths. That processing dominance lets export restrictions ripple into nonmilitary production, including the source's example of Ford Explorer manufacturing pausing in Chicago after a heavy rare earth supply disruption.

[[battlefield-rare-earths-how-the-us-lost-to-china]] adds the U.S. industry history behind the same bottleneck. The episode uses [[MountainPassRareEarthMine]], [[Molycorp]], [[MarkSmith]], [[ProjectPhoenix]], and [[MPMaterials]] to show that domestic mining can exist without durable mineral security if separation, refining, customer demand, finance, and price resilience remain weak. It also adds [[RareEarthExportLeverage]] and [[DominantProducerPriceDiscipline]] as two ways processing dominance can become geopolitical power.

## Key Claims
- Mineral security depends on processing capacity and partner networks, not only on geology or formal ownership of deposits.
- Heavy rare earths are especially important because the source says the U.S. has more light rare earths and lacks the same capability to turn heavy rare earths into needed components.
- [[Greenland]] can matter strategically while still being too slow and costly to solve immediate supply-chain exposure.
- [[Brazil]], [[Australia]], [[SaudiArabia|Saudi Arabia]], and [[India]] are named as more plausible near-term sources or processing-technology partners.
- [[battlefield-rare-earths-how-the-us-lost-to-china]] shows the bottleneck historically: the [[UnitedStates]] once had major Mountain Pass capacity but lost the processing and market structure needed to withstand cheaper Chinese supply.
- The source adds that rebuilding rare-earth security may require [[StateBackedRareEarthRebuilding]], including loans, grants, equity stakes, and price-floor coordination.
