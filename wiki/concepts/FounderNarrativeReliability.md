# Founder Narrative Reliability

Founder narrative reliability is the source-reading problem made explicit in [[no-203-businiao-lanshili-979295393]]. [[Banlatte]] repeatedly notes that [[LanShili]] has written and said a great deal about his own life, but some amounts, motives, and timelines are hard to verify or vary across accounts.

The concept is useful because founder stories are often compelling precisely when they compress uncertain facts into a clean arc. This episode resists that compression by placing Lan's self-narration beside media reporting, court judgments, company announcements, official notices, [[YuanShanla]]'s responses, [[RongzhongGroup]]'s position, and the criminal-court distinction between acquittal and civil dispute.

## Key Claims
- A founder's account can be valuable evidence of perception, motive, and self-image without being sufficient proof of facts.
- Large numbers, dramatic timing, and post-crisis blame claims deserve extra caution when independent records are thin.
- Court findings answer specific legal questions and should not be overextended into full moral or business judgments.
- Later official punishment of one actor does not automatically validate every earlier accusation made by a founder.
