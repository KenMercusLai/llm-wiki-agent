# Microhistory From Hostile Archives

Microhistory from hostile archives names the method problem foregrounded by [[148-wangshi-zhisi-duanjing-tuiyuan-shang-de-lishi-zhuangjing-892588934]]. [[JonathanSpence|史景迁]]'s [[TheDeathOfWomanWang|《王氏之死》]] tries to reconstruct [[WangShiTancheng|王氏]] and [[Tancheng|郯城]] from records that were not made to preserve her inner life: county gazetteer entries, magistrate case practice, tax and disaster records, household shame, and literary analogies.

The archive is "hostile" in the practical sense that it records poor women mainly when they become legal, moral, or administrative problems. The method therefore depends on careful triangulation. [[TanchengCountyGazetteer|《郯城县志》]] supplies disaster and local structure, [[FuhuiQuanshu|《福惠全书》]] supplies magistrate procedure, and [[LiaozhaiZhiyi|《聊斋志异》]] supplies social imagination. The result is not direct recovery of Wang's voice, but a disciplined reconstruction of the constraints that made her voice hard to record.

## Key Claims
- Marginal lives often appear in records only through crime, punishment, taxation, reputation, or family conflict.
- Microhistory can make a small case historically serious by reconstructing the surrounding world of institutions, scarcity, and belief.
- Fiction and folklore can help map imagination and anxiety, but they must be separated from direct factual proof.
- The method is strongest when it marks inference clearly, especially around unnamed or illiterate subjects.
