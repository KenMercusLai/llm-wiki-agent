# Financial Literacy Measurement

Financial literacy measurement is the use of survey questions to assess what people know about personal finance, risk, debt, saving, or related economic decisions. In [[tech-20260209-0209-mp-tech-pod-128-tech-20260209-0209-mp-tech-pod-128]], the concept becomes a cautionary case: [[FINRAFoundation]] survey data showed a 15% decline in personal-finance knowledge from 2009 to 2021, but [[CarlyUrban]] argues that device changes may explain part of that decline.

The source does not say financial knowledge is impossible to measure. It says measurement needs design discipline: device disclosure, response-time analysis, question ordering, and explicit treatment of [[SurveyModeEffects]] before survey results guide education policy or cross-country rankings.

## Key Claims
- A knowledge trend can look like a public-education failure even when the survey environment has changed.
- Phones can reduce measured knowledge performance without necessarily reducing the respondent's real-world financial capability.
- Knowledge questions behave differently from questions about the respondent's own life or demographics.
- Incentivizing correct answers can create a lookup problem, so effort is hard to separate from outside assistance.
- Future chatbot access may make financial-knowledge measures harder to interpret unless surveys distinguish memory, reasoning, search, and AI assistance.
