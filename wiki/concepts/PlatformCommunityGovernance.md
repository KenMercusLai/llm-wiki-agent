# Platform Community Governance

Platform community governance is the operating problem of managing a participatory platform whose users, moderators, norms, and abuse patterns can determine whether the product remains healthy. [[socialradarspod-stevehuffman-final]] adds the concept through [[Reddit]]'s 2015 crisis: [[SteveHuffman]] says the platform was in the press for the wrong reasons, the team was in disarray, and a small group of abusive users was tormenting the broader community.

The crisis escalated after Reddit fired a community-beloved moderator liaison, leading moderators to take communities private. The source frames governance as a legitimacy problem, not only a policy problem: Reddit's moderators and users had enough power to make the platform's failure visible, and Huffman's return depended on the belief that founder credibility and product memory could help stabilize it.

## Key Claims
- User-powered platforms need governance capacity proportional to community power.
- Moderators are not external helpers; they can become core infrastructure whose trust affects platform availability.
- Abuse problems can be concentrated in a small group while still shaping the whole platform's reputation and experience.
- Governance failures can become leadership crises when internal teams cannot explain or repair decisions that anger the community.
