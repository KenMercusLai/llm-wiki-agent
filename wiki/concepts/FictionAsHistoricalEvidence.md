# Fiction As Historical Evidence

Fiction as historical evidence is the source-method question raised by [[148-wangshi-zhisi-duanjing-tuiyuan-shang-de-lishi-zhuangjing-892588934]]. [[JonathanSpence|史景迁]] uses [[PuSongling|蒲松龄]]'s [[LiaozhaiZhiyi|《聊斋志异》]] beside [[TanchengCountyGazetteer|《郯城县志》]] and [[FuhuiQuanshu|《福惠全书》]] in [[TheDeathOfWomanWang|《王氏之死》]], which makes the book controversial and methodologically useful at the same time.

The concept's boundary is that fiction is not treated as a transcript of events. In this source, ghost stories, fox women, miraculous women, wife-sale stories, bandit-household satire, and fantasies of revenge reveal what could be feared, desired, mocked, or morally imagined in Qing local society. That makes fiction a source for social imagination, not a shortcut around ordinary evidence.

## Key Claims
- Fiction can preserve fears, norms, fantasies, and moral tensions that official records omit.
- Fiction is weakest when used as direct proof that an event happened.
- Fiction becomes stronger as evidence when it is checked against place, law, administrative records, material conditions, and recurring social patterns.
- The method helps explain why [[LiaozhaiZhiyi|《聊斋志异》]] can illuminate [[WangShiTancheng|王氏]]'s world without pretending to speak in her exact voice.
