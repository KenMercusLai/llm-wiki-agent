# Status-Quo Rationalization / 现状合理化

Status-quo rationalization / 现状合理化 is the episode's explanation for why obedience can feel psychologically easier than dissent. In [[116-bufucong-de-yishu-fenxiang-yixie-shizhan-fangfa-haiyou-liliang-764416021]], the hosts describe experiments where people rate long-existing art, interrogation methods, acupuncture, political leadership, or institutions more favorably when those things feel historically established or unavoidable.

The source's main point is not that people are stupid. It is that humans often defend current systems to preserve stability, control, group belonging, and hope. When exit feels impossible or the group feels threatened, unfair arrangements can become easier to justify because uncertainty feels worse.

## Key Claims
- The existing order can acquire a false aura of reasonableness simply because it already exists.
- Threat can increase conformity by making criticism of an institution feel like criticism of the group itself.
- Low exit can intensify rationalization: if people believe they cannot leave a system, they may defend it more strongly.
- Hope is double-edged because it can sustain people through difficulty while also helping them endure or excuse harmful arrangements.
- Understanding this bias should create compassion and strategy, not contempt for people who obey.
