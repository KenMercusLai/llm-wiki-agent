# Supply Chain Sovereignty

Supply chain sovereignty is the policy idea that the [[UnitedStates]] should depend less on vulnerable foreign production and more on domestic workers, allies, and strategically controlled inputs. [[vol-113-cong-jiqian-ye-zhiku-wenjian-zhong-goule-telangpu-2-0-zhizheng-jihua-beihou-de-ren-jigou-sixiang-he-mailuo-lihnknc-3om5evexocb6a6zdwueu]] draws this mainly from the [[AmericaFirstPolicyInstitute]] agenda and connects it to the pandemic, war, floods, energy supply, trade deficits, and industrial capacity.

The source is skeptical that full self-sufficiency is realistic across the economy. It argues that energy is the area where the United States can most plausibly pursue greater self-reliance, while manufacturing supply chains and advanced industrial ecosystems remain more complex.

[[ep270-yi-mei-xinpian-de-manchang-zhengtu-women-li-suanli-ziyou-haiyou-duoyuan-lm7lxlmcnjwnawtq-9typc-fnrci]] adds a Chinese semiconductor version. The episode frames AI-chip substitution as a need for domestic closure across [[ElectronicDesignAutomation|EDA]], wafer fabrication, lithography, materials, packaging, testing, software ecosystems, power, and cost-effective scale, rather than a simple decision to replace one imported chip.

## Key Claims
- Supply-chain resilience is used to justify domestic production, ally-shoring, energy expansion, tariffs, and trade reciprocity.
- The concept links economic policy to security policy: a trade deficit becomes a sign of outsourced manufacturing and defense-industrial weakness.
- The source notes a tension between anti-inflation goals and reshoring or tariff policies, because protection can raise near-term prices.
- [[PeterNavarro]]'s trade-deficit argument is one of the clearest examples of this frame in the source.
- Semiconductor sovereignty is harder than a single-company success story because tools, materials, equipment, process know-how, yield, and downstream software adoption all have to mature together.
