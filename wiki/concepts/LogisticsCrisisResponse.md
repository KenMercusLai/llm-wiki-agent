# Logistics Crisis Response

Logistics crisis response is the operating pattern where a logistics company becomes more valuable when physical infrastructure, geopolitics, regulation, or demand shocks break normal shipping assumptions. In [[tsr-s3-ryanpetersen-v6-tsr-s3-ryanpetersen-v6]], [[RyanPetersen]] presents [[Flexport]]'s responses to pandemic PPE shortages, Red Sea disruption, Long Beach port congestion, and the Ever Given incident as examples.

[[how-to-get-through-the-strait-of-hormuz]] adds the [[StraitOfHormuz]] crisis as a source where response is partly informational and legal rather than rerouting alone. The episode shows cargo owners trying to learn whether goods are on a damaged ship, whether a different ship has loaded them, whether passage requires permission, and whether [[GeneralAverage]] could create shared costs.

The source shows several response modes. During COVID, Flexport.org handled humanitarian shipping and Flexport borrowed passenger planes to move PPE when passenger-flight belly cargo disappeared. During Red Sea attacks, ships turned off transponders, so Flexport rebuilt parts of its user experience to identify diverted containers and estimate arrival times. At Long Beach, Petersen gathered field evidence from port workers and trucking operators, then used a public tweetstorm to accelerate official attention to container-stacking limits.

The concept is not simply "move fast in a crisis." It depends on [[GlobalLogisticsCoordination]] and [[LogisticsWorkflowAutomation]] already being in place: data visibility, field access, carrier relationships, and workflow changes let the company translate chaos into customer-facing information and new routes.

## Key Claims
- Supply-chain crises turn visibility, routing knowledge, and operational credibility into customer value.
- A logistics crisis can require both software changes and physical capacity changes.
- Field investigation can reveal bottlenecks that dashboard-level views miss.
- Public explanation can influence policy attention, but it may not solve a crisis whose root cause is demand or capacity normalization.
