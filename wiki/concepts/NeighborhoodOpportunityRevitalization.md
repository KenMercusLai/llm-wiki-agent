# Neighborhood Opportunity Revitalization

Neighborhood opportunity revitalization is the place-based idea that policy can improve low-income children's long-term outcomes by changing or integrating the neighborhood around them, rather than only moving families somewhere else. [[can-transforming-neighborhoods-help-kids-escape-poverty]] develops it through [[HOPEVIProgram|HOPE VI]], [[RichardAllenHomes]], and [[RajChetty]]'s evidence on adult outcomes for children exposed to revitalized public housing.

The concept is narrower than generic redevelopment. The source says children benefited mainly when revitalized public housing connected them to more affluent surrounding communities, making [[CrossClassSocialCapital]] more important than newer buildings by themselves.

## Key Claims
- Place improvement can be an anti-poverty strategy when it changes the opportunity environment children actually experience.
- The mechanism depends on nearby higher-opportunity communities, not only better units or prettier design.
- Adult residents saw limited gains, while children exposed for more years saw larger later effects.
- Revitalization must be evaluated alongside [[PublicHousingDisplacement]] because the same program can help some children while pushing out other families.
