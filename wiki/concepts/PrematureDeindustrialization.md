# Premature Deindustrialization

Premature deindustrialization is the pattern where countries begin losing manufacturing's growth and employment role before they have become rich. In [[the-giant-factory-town-that-might-be-a-giant-mistake]], [[MayaraFelix]] says modern manufacturing is more technology-intensive, employs fewer people, and is hard to compete in against [[China]].

The concept is central to the episode's warning about the old development blueprint. If manufacturing no longer absorbs large numbers of workers or no longer provides a clear upgrading ladder, countries such as [[Brazil]] need productivity gains from other sectors, not just more factories.

[[why-are-we-so-obsessed-with-manufacturing]] adds the high-income-country labor version. The [[PlanetMoney]] episode says manufacturing employment has been declining around the world largely because of automation, which means the [[UnitedStates]] cannot solve [[GoodJobsForNonCollegeWorkers]] by treating factory revival as the only jobs strategy.

## Key Claims
- Manufacturing can shrink as an employment path before a country reaches high-income status.
- Automation and global competition make the older factory-led ladder harder to climb.
- The source connects the pattern to [[Brazil]]'s stalled growth after earlier rapid industrialization.
- Escaping the pattern may require [[AdvancedAgricultureInnovation]], service-sector innovation, and [[LocalizedInnovationAdvantage]].
- In the U.S. labor-market frame, automation makes [[ManufacturingPayPremium]] valuable but insufficient because fewer factory jobs may be available even when each job is attractive.
