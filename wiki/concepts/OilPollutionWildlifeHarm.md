# Oil-Pollution Wildlife Harm

Oil-pollution wildlife harm is the source's concrete image for ecological externality: crude oil reaches living bodies that did not create the industrial system. In [[138-qie-ke-wo-zai-haitan-shang-jiandao-yiwei-q-laoshi-842331726]], [[TomMichell|Tom Michell / 汤姆·米切尔]] finds a beach in [[Uruguay]] covered with dead or dying [[MagellanicPenguin|Magellanic penguins / 麦哲伦企鹅]], then rescues the one later named [[JuanSalvadorPenguin|Juan Salvador / 胡安·萨尔瓦多]].

The concept extends [[EnvironmentalTradeoffAccounting]] by making the displaced cost bodily rather than abstract. Oil is not just a commodity or transport input in the episode; it becomes sticky weight, cold exposure, lost waterproofing, hunger, failed mobility, and mass death. The source's closing argument ties that harm to [[EmpathyCircleExpansion]]: some beings pay for human systems with lives because they have no other currency.

## Key Claims
- Pollution can convert an ordinary habitat into a mass-casualty scene before most humans see the cost.
- Wildlife harm is not only population-level; it can be felt through one animal's struggle, cleaning, hunger, and uncertain recovery.
- Cleanup and rescue do not erase the underlying externality, because the harmed animals did not choose or price the risk.
- Ecological harm should be tracked through vulnerable bodies as well as through policy, commodity, or industry categories.
- The concept makes [[WildAnimalRescueResponsibility]] necessary but also shows why rescue is a downstream response, not a substitute for prevention.
