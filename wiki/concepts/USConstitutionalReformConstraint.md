# U.S. Constitutional Reform Constraint

U.S. constitutional reform constraint is the episode's explanation for why many American institutional problems are visible but hard to change. In [[sp-05-liuqing-linyao-qiangsha-yibao-jutou-an-beihou-de-sikao-xia-fenlie-de-meiguo-hui-you-xianzheng-weiji-ma-790241978]], [[LinYao|林垚]] emphasizes the Article V amendment threshold: two-thirds of both houses of Congress and ratification by three-fourths of the states.

The source links that threshold to the founding compromise with slaveholding states and then to the present. The [[UnitedStatesConstitution]] can look stable because amendment is difficult, but the same difficulty can trap old institutional choices such as the Electoral College, equal state representation in the Senate, and the [[PresidentialSystem|presidential system]].

## Key Claims
- High amendment thresholds can protect a constitution from opportunistic revision while also making structural repair nearly impossible under polarization.
- The source treats the Civil War as evidence that slavery was not removed by normal amendment alone; it took military rupture before constitutional text could catch up.
- Progressive-era amendments show that reform is possible, but often only when social movements, crises, and contingent events align.
- [[NationalPopularVoteInterstateCompact]] is presented as a partial workaround for the Electoral College, not a full solution to presidential-system risk.
- Local experimentation has become weaker as federal fiscal power, federal law, and [[SupremeCourt]] review have expanded.
