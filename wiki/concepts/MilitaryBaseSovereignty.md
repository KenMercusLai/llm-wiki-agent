# Military Base Sovereignty

Military base sovereignty is the pattern in [[how-to-get-what-greenland-has-with-permission]] where a state gains strategic control through bases, jurisdiction, and access rights rather than formal annexation. [[DanielImmerwahr]] uses the idea to explain why modern expansion can be quieter than conquest or purchase.

The source's clearest example is [[GuantanamoBay]]: it is in [[Cuba]], Cuba has asked the [[UnitedStates]] to leave, and the U.S. has not left. The episode also notes that flags, soil, host-country rules, and jurisdiction can become politically sensitive when a base starts to look like sovereignty in practice.

For Greenland, the concept matters because the U.S. already has extensive defense access under long-running arrangements with [[Greenland]] and [[Denmark]]. That makes the practical strategic question less about buying the island and more about whether existing or expanded access can serve warning, missile-defense, and space-surveillance needs without destabilizing allied relations.
