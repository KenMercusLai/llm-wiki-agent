# Forensic Hair Evidence

Forensic hair evidence is the use of hair as a trace in criminal investigation, toxicology, DNA testing, and historical identification. [[93-liaoliao-toufa-tu-le-jiu-hui-bian-qiang-ma-718794489]] introduces the idea through [[EdgarAllanPoe|Edgar Allan Poe / 爱伦·坡]]'s "The Murders in the Rue Morgue," then distinguishes modern DNA and toxicology value from the weaker older practice of visual hair comparison.

The concept strengthens [[ContestedForensicAttribution]]. Hair is useful because it is shed easily and may preserve biological or chemical information. But the [[DonaldGates]] case shows that treating hair shape or color as decisive identification can help convict the wrong person, especially when expert testimony, informants, or narrative pressure outrun the evidence.

## Key Claims
- Hair can be valuable physical evidence because it is hard to avoid shedding and can carry biological or chemical traces.
- Hair evidence becomes dangerous when visual comparison is presented as stronger than its actual identification power.
- DNA and toxicology can improve what hair can show, but contamination, sample handling, and inference discipline still matter.
- Hair evidence should be interpreted with [[ScientificSamplingDiscipline]] and separated from suspect narratives.
