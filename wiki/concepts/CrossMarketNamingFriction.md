# Cross-Market Naming Friction

Cross-market naming friction is the localization problem that appears when a product name works in one market but loses recognition, rhythm, or meaning in another. In [[board-games-3-whats-in-a-name]], [[SellMeASasquatch]] works for the U.S. team because Sasquatch, Bigfoot, Yeti, and paranormal nostalgia feel familiar and funny. International teams worry that European shoppers may not know the word Sasquatch.

The source treats the issue as operational as well as linguistic. Launching under different names could require separate art, naming, production setup, and retailer coordination, while translating the word could weaken the joke that made the U.S. version appealing.

## Key Claims
- The strongest local name can become a global rollout constraint.
- Localization is not only translation; it affects art, packaging, production timelines, retailer pitches, and brand consistency.
- A culturally specific reference can create [[RetailShelfAppeal]] where it is known and confusion where it is not.
- The cost of solving naming friction can push a team to prioritize one market first and let other markets adapt later.
