# Asymmetric Information

Asymmetric information is a market condition where one side of a transaction knows more than the other about the product, risk, or quality being exchanged. In [[seven-allegedly-fake-chanel-bags-vs-the-realreal]], buyers of secondhand luxury goods cannot easily know whether a used Chanel bag is genuine, while sellers, brands, and specialists may have more provenance or inspection knowledge.

The episode uses [[TheRealReal]] to show one marketplace response: move the hard judgment from the buyer to an authentication operation. That connects asymmetric information to [[AuthenticationLedMarketplaceTrust]], [[LuxuryResaleAuthentication]], and [[MarketplaceFrictionReduction]] because reducing uncertainty can be the product, not just a background feature.

[[board-games-3-whats-in-a-name]] adds the playable version through [[MarketForLemons|The Market for Lemons]] and [[SellMeASasquatch]]. Instead of using asymmetric information only to explain market failure or authentication, the episode turns hidden quality into a party-game mechanic where uncertainty, bluffing, and distrust are the fun.

## Key Claims
- Information gaps can block otherwise valuable trades because buyers discount or avoid goods they cannot verify.
- A platform can reduce asymmetric information by standardizing inspection, warranties, dispute handling, and presentation.
- The same trust layer can create legal exposure if the platform makes stronger claims than it can prove.
- Incumbent brands may have superior internal data, but giving them exclusive authority can create competition and access problems.
- Asymmetric information can also become an experiential teaching device when [[GameMechanicToRetailProduct]] turns theory into structured play.
