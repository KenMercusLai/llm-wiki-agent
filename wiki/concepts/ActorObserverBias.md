# Actor-Observer Bias / 行动者-观察者偏差

Actor-observer bias is the tendency for actors to explain their own behavior through circumstances while observers explain the same kind of behavior through character. [[123-renxing-shiyan-dangxin-ni-ziji-779479569]] introduces the concept through ordinary queue conflict, a return dispute, Watergate-era political attribution, and a staged "criminal plan" study.

The episode's compressed formula is that other people seem to have traits while "I" have situations. That makes the bias ethically important: it can turn poverty, victimization, anger, or mistake into a whole-person defect when the observer has not examined the person's constraints.

## Key Claims
- Actors have access to preceding context, pressure, and internal hesitation; observers mainly see the person acting now.
- Observers often underestimate situational force and overstate stable personality.
- Compassionate context does not erase responsibility; the source warns against both cruel character judgment and unlimited externalization.
- The bias is one mechanism behind online condemnation and weak-position blame.
