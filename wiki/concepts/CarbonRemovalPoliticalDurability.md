# Carbon Removal Political Durability

Carbon removal political durability is the ability of [[CarbonRemoval]] projects to retain support across partisan or administrative shifts. [[tech-20260713-tech-pod-128-tech-20260713-tech-pod-128]] adds the concept through [[AaronBurns]] of [[Carbon180]], who says the Trump administration rescinded funding for several Biden-era [[DirectAirCapture]] projects while [[UnitedStatesCongress|Congress]] protected planned hubs in [[Louisiana]] and [[Texas]].

The episode suggests that direct air capture can survive where climate, industrial policy, state economic interest, and corporate procurement overlap. That durability is not purely positive: the same coalition logic can intensify [[CarbonRemovalMoralHazard]] when fossil-fuel companies support projects or when removal is framed as a way to avoid harder emissions cuts.

## Key Claims
- A climate technology can have different political prospects depending on host-state jobs, infrastructure, corporate buyers, and industry allies.
- Congressional protection can keep specific hubs alive even when broader climate funding is cut.
- Durability built on fossil-fuel support may preserve projects while weakening public trust.
- Political durability is different from climate effectiveness; projects still need low costs, clean energy, and permanent storage.
