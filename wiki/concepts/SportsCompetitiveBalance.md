# Sports Competitive Balance

[[how-we-got-free-agents-in-baseball]] adds the labor-control version of competitive balance. [[MajorLeagueBaseball|MLB]] defended restrictions on player movement partly by arguing that a freer player market could let one team buy too much talent, while the source uses the [[LosAngelesDodgers|Los Angeles Dodgers]] to show why that debate still returns after [[BaseballFreeAgency|free agency]].

Sports competitive balance is the league-design goal of giving weak teams enough hope to improve while preserving credible competition across the league. [[planet-money-vs-the-nbas-tanking-problem]] makes the goal concrete through the [[NationalBasketballAssociation|NBA]] draft: high picks can help bad teams rebuild, but the same mechanism can create [[SportsTanking]].

The source shows that competitive balance is not a single rule. It is a shifting compromise among draft order, lottery odds, free-agent market power, small-market confidence, fan trust, and [[SportsPlayerAutonomy]].

## Key Claims
- Leagues need weak teams to have a plausible recovery path.
- Over-rewarding weakness can make losing rational.
- Under-rewarding weakness can leave bad teams stuck and fans without hope.
- Market freedom can improve player autonomy while raising long-run inequality concerns.
- Competitive balance therefore depends on [[LeagueStakeholderAlignment]], not only talent distribution.
- Competitive-balance arguments need a labor-power audit because the same rules that stabilize league outcomes can suppress player exit and wages.
