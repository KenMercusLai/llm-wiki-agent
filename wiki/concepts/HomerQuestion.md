# Homer Question

The Homer question is the authorship and formation problem around [[Homer]], [[TheIliad|《伊利亚特》]], and [[TheOdyssey]]. In [[96-hema-shishi-zai-jiazhuang-yongsheng-de-shidai-women-zhongdu-siwang-yiliyate-pian-725317514]], [[MihuanChishu|蜜獾吃书]] presents it as a live interpretive issue: Homer may be one person, many singers, a later organizing name, male, female, blind, or partly legendary.

The episode also distinguishes this from a newer question about historical memory: whether the epics preserve fragments of Mycenaean custom, ritual, feasting, theology, and social order. The point is not to solve the problem in the episode, but to make readers aware that [[OralFormulaicEpic|oral-formulaic epic]] and later textual arrangement shape what "the author" can mean.

[[ep269-cong-manwei-yuzhou-dao-aodesai-weihe-zongyao-fanpai-yingxiong-shishi-ltklotheqpup6oamyflfzpxtipsw]] reinforces the transmission side by putting Homer beside [[TheEpicOfGilgamesh|Gilgamesh]] and [[PoeticEdda|Edda]] material. [[LiuChang|刘畅]] stresses that fixed epithets and repeated formulas make more sense when [[TheIliad|《伊利亚特》]] and [[TheOdyssey|《奥德赛》]] are heard as performance-shaped works rather than only as later silent-reading classics.

## Key Claims
- Homeric authorship should be treated as a formation problem, not only as a biography question.
- Oral performance, small-song combination, and later editing can coexist with extraordinary literary coherence.
- Historical echoes inside the epics are not automatically documentary evidence.
- The problem supports [[ClassicReadingComplexity]] because textual authority, tradition, and source uncertainty must be held together.
- Cross-epic comparison makes the Homer question less exceptional: oral performance, manuscript preservation, and later organization also shape [[TheEpicOfGilgamesh]] and [[PoeticEdda]].
