# Hard-Tech Customer Intent Proof

Hard-tech customer intent proof is the pattern where a future buyer's credible public interest helps a capital-intensive startup look real before the product can be delivered. [[tsr-s5-blakescholl-v3-finalaudio-tsr-s5-blakescholl-v3-finalaudio]] adds the concept through [[BoomSupersonic]] and the [[VirginGroup|Virgin]] signal before [[YCombinator]] Demo Day.

Boom could not show software-style traction for a supersonic airliner. [[MichaelSeibel]] challenged [[BlakeScholl]] on whether a weak startup-airline LOI was enough, so Scholl pursued [[RichardBranson]] and asked not for money but for permission to announce that Virgin wanted the first aircraft when they flew. The episode treats that statement as a credibility bridge between renderings, hardware, and future orders.

The concept is weaker than a completed sale. Intent can be strategic, nonbinding, or conditional. Its value comes from making the de-risking path legible to investors, employees, press, and later customers while the company is still building proof hardware and technical capability.

[[tsr-s5-davidkirtley-v2-audio-tsr-s5-davidkirtley-v2-audio]] adds the commercial-fusion version through [[Helion]]. [[DavidKirtley]] treats the [[Microsoft]] power-purchase agreement, including accountability and possible penalties, as evidence that serious customers should hold hard-tech companies to delivery promises. The [[Nucor]] plan adds a second customer pattern: behind-the-meter industrial power for a steel manufacturing site.

## Key Claims
- For long-cycle hard tech, credible customer intent can matter before product delivery, revenue, or production usage exist.
- The strongest intent signals come from buyers whose brand, history, or operating need makes the interest plausible.
- Customer intent has to be paired with technical proof; otherwise it can become another version of pitch theater.
- Delivery accountability makes customer intent stronger because the commitment tests timelines, permitting, engineering, and operating risk rather than only public interest.
