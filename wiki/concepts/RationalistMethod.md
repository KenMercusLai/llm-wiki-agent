# Rationalist Method

Rationalist method is the episode's nameable pattern for [[ReneDescartes|Descartes]]: begin from clear intuition or an indubitable starting point, then use logical deduction to build knowledge. In [[174-du-dikaer-shi-jian-dashi-966856702]], this is explained through [[MethodicDoubt]], [[CogitoErgoSum]], mathematical examples, syllogism, and the analogy to Euclidean geometry.

The source does not present this as the whole of science. It contrasts rationalist deduction with empirical induction and says modern inquiry needs both, while still treating Descartes's mathematical style as a major contribution to modern scientific thinking.

[[91-liewu-sailemu-1692-cong-yizheng-kaishi-714732018]] adds a practical anti-witch-hunt use. After discussing the [[SalemWitchTrials|塞勒姆猎巫]], the episode invokes [[ReneDescartes|笛卡尔]] as a way to resist pseudo-causality: do not accept a claim too quickly, divide complex questions, move from simpler checks to harder conclusions, and review for omissions.

## Key Claims
- Clear starting points matter because unchecked inherited belief produces confusion.
- Deduction can be powerful when the premise is secure, as in mathematical reasoning.
- The source separates rational construction from blind certainty: a system can be elegant and still need humility about what its premises prove.
- Empirical induction is not rejected; it is the other route that natural science also requires.
- The life-practice extension is to find a usable origin point before acting, not to delay all action until absolute certainty.
- The Salem extension uses rationalist discipline against [[WitchHuntMechanism]]: a causal story about hidden enemies is not enough unless the evidentiary steps survive examination.
