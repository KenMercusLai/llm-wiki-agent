# Publication Bias

Publication bias is the selection problem in [[dont-hate-the-replicator-hate-the-game]] where journals and researchers are more likely to publish novel, statistically significant findings than null, ambiguous, or failed results. In the episode, [[AbelBrodeur]] sees this directly when his smoking-ban paper becomes harder to place because the straightforward result is no effect.

The concept matters because it can distort the visible literature even when individual researchers are not fabricating data. If near-miss or null studies remain unpublished while significant-looking findings are polished and submitted, readers see a cleaner and stronger evidence base than the underlying research process produced.

## Key Claims
- Publication bias can make the published record overstate effect sizes or certainty.
- Null results are evidence, but the source says they often have weaker publication incentives.
- Bias at the submission and acceptance stage can interact with [[PHacking]] because researchers learn what kinds of results are rewarded.
- A visible bunching of results near a [[StatisticalSignificanceThreshold]] can be a warning sign that the literature has been filtered or tuned.
- [[ReplicationGames]] address publication bias indirectly by increasing the chance that fragile published results will be checked.
