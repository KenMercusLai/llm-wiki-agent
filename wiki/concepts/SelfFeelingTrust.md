# 相信自己的感受 / Self-Feeling Trust

Self-feeling trust is the source's countermeasure to always looking for the standard answer. In [[140-haikeyi-de-jin-nvshi-suoyi-ren-weishenme-yao-nuli-a-854173655]], [[Jinzi|金子]] describes reading the Neapolitan novels and wanting to confirm whether her feeling about a scene was "right"; a writer friend replies that the feeling a literary work gives the reader is already an answer.

The concept extends beyond literary interpretation. The episode uses the same habit to explain why people distrust their own response to hardship, rest, exercise, fashion, effort, and life choices. If education has trained a person to wait for the teacher's answer key, then adult self-direction requires learning to treat feeling as evidence without treating it as infallible.

[[187-wandou-gongzhu-gaomin-he-hundun-shi-ni-zhenming-999958166]] adds a sensory version. The person who says a bed, heat, texture, sound, or small stimulus is unbearable may be reporting real data from their body, even when bystanders prefer to call the response "矫情." The source does not make feeling infallible; it asks that feeling be admitted before judgment.

## Key Claims
- A feeling is not automatically a final truth, but it is valid data that should not be discarded before interpretation begins.
- Standard-answer training can make people outsource judgment even in domains where no answer key exists.
- Literature is a useful practice field because it legitimizes response, ambiguity, and partial understanding.
- Self-feeling trust differs from impulsiveness: the point is to include felt response in judgment, not to stop thinking.
- The source links this trust to bodily and social decisions: if hardship, beauty, respectability, and effort all feel wrong, that feeling deserves inquiry before obedience.
- Sensory discomfort is also a form of felt evidence; dismissing it too quickly can reproduce shame around bodies that perceive differently.
