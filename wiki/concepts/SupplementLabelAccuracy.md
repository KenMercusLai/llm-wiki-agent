# Supplement Label Accuracy

Supplement label accuracy is the problem in [[why-is-there-a-supplement-craze-if-they-dont-even-work]] that the ingredient list and amounts on a bottle may not reliably match what is inside. [[MarionNestle]] says food labels are more reliable because the [[FoodAndDrugAdministration|FDA]] sometimes spot-checks food ingredients, while supplement consumers may have less practical assurance.

The source makes this problem concrete through [[ConsumerLab]], which reportedly found wide variation across turmeric, echinacea, and elderberry products. Label accuracy is separate from efficacy: even before asking whether a supplement works, the consumer may need to ask whether the product contains what the package says.

## Key Claims
- Label accuracy is a first-order consumer-risk issue, not a minor technicality.
- Independent testing can reveal missing, under-dosed, over-dosed, or non-authentic ingredients.
- Heavy metals or other contaminants can turn a weak-evidence product into a safety concern.
- [[ThirdPartySupplementTesting]] can reduce uncertainty about contents but cannot prove broad health benefits.
