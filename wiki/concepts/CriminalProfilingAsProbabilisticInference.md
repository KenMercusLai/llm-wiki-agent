# Criminal Profiling As Probabilistic Inference

Criminal profiling as probabilistic inference is the source's cautious frame for using behavior, communications, crime-scene signatures, and psychological expertise to guide investigation. [[121-jijian-fayi-jianshi-shouce-yiqie-fanzui-jieyou-henji-775064343]] uses [[JamesBrussel]] and the "Mad Bomber" case to show why profiling can be useful, while the broader episode keeps it inside [[ForensicScience]] rather than above it.

The same source uses [[KennethBianchi]] to show a neighboring forensic-psychology problem: suspects can perform or feign symptoms, and experts can be tested by adversarial legal strategy. That makes psychological evidence useful but bounded. Profiles and diagnoses should generate hypotheses, guide search, or test claims; they should not replace physical evidence and corroboration.

## Key Claims
- Profiles can help prioritize search, but fit is not proof.
- Crime signatures and trophies can reveal behavioral patterns, but they still need grounding in case evidence.
- Mental illness claims, memory claims, and suspect performances require careful testing.
- Psychological interpretation should remain accountable to [[EvidenceOverTestimony]], [[ObservationBeforeInference]], and [[RationalHumility]].
