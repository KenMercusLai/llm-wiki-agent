# Brand-Controlled Authentication

Brand-controlled authentication is the claim that the original brand is the only party with enough product, manufacturing, and database knowledge to decide whether an item is genuine. In [[seven-allegedly-fake-chanel-bags-vs-the-realreal]], [[Chanel]] advances this position against [[TheRealReal]] by arguing that only Chanel can know what is authentic Chanel.

The concept is persuasive because brands may have internal serial-number records, manufacturing details, supplier changes, and quality-control history that outsiders lack. It is also risky because exclusive brand authority over authenticity can become a way to limit [[FirstSaleDoctrine]] resale and preserve direct-channel control.

## Key Claims
- Brand knowledge can improve authentication when counterfeiters reproduce visible details well.
- Exclusive brand control can weaken independent resale markets by making third-party authentication legally fragile.
- The practical issue is not whether brand knowledge matters, but whether it should be the only recognized path to authenticity.
- Brand-controlled authentication turns trust into a governance question: consumer protection, trademark value, and market access can point in different directions.
