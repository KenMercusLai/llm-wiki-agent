# Permit Proportionality

Permit proportionality is the requirement that a government permit condition be reasonably tied to the harm or impact the permit is meant to address. [[its-my-tree-why-cant-i-cut-it-down]] makes this the decisive legal frame in the [[CantonMichigan]] tree case.

The source's key distinction is between regulating tree removal and using a one-size-fits-all fee formula. Canton could argue that tree removal harms [[UrbanCanopyExternalities]], but the Sixth Circuit account in the episode found the fee system unconstitutional as applied because it did not adequately account for each tree's specific ecological value or community benefit.

## Key Claims
- Proportionality does not require cities to ignore environmental harms; it requires them to connect the demanded mitigation to the specific harm.
- Standardized fees are vulnerable when they treat dissimilar trees, parcels, and ecological contexts as equivalent.
- Individualized arborist valuation is one way to make a tree-removal fee more defensible.
- Proportionality can preserve room for [[TreeProtectionOrdinances]] while preventing public costs from being loaded arbitrarily onto individual owners.
- The concept is a bridge between [[ExternalityInternalization]] and [[RegulatoryTakings]]: price the spillover, but show the price is grounded.
