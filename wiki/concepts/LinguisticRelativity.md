# Linguistic Relativity

Linguistic relativity is the idea that language habits can shape cognition. In [[104-ni-yisheng-de-gushi-zhongdian-yijing-zhuding-haiyao-chufa-ma-739384680]], the hosts discuss it through the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis while noting that the strong version is contested. The episode uses [[StoryOfYourLife|《你一生的故事》]] as a fictional extreme: learning [[HeptapodB]] changes how [[LouiseBanks|Louise Banks / 路易斯·班克斯]] experiences time.

The concept links practical language awareness to speculative literature. The source compares spoken language, writing, Chinese, classical Chinese, and dialects to show that language systems do not simply label preexisting thoughts. They guide attention, category habits, and what a speaker finds natural.

## Key Claims
- Language can shape perception and thought without needing to determine every possible thought.
- Speech and writing may organize cognition differently.
- Fiction can push a contested linguistic idea to an extreme in order to test its philosophical stakes.
- In the Chiang story, the language-change question becomes inseparable from [[NonlinearTimePerception]] and [[PredestinedChoice]].
