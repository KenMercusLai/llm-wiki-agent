# Painful Memory Rehearsal Risk / 痛苦记忆反复咀嚼风险

Painful memory rehearsal risk is the episode's caution that repeatedly replaying a painful event can help make that memory more durable. In [[143-yiwang-de-naoli-jixing-buhao-ke-zha-zheng-a-869667634]], the hosts apply the same consolidation logic used for studying to negative experience: if repetition strengthens learning, repeated rumination can also strengthen distressing memory.

The concept is source-scoped and non-clinical. It does not mean people should deny injury, force cheerfulness, or avoid treatment. It marks the episode's practical distinction between allowing low mood and repeatedly rehearsing the harmful scene itself; the suggested counterweight is gentle new experience, movement, absorption, and time for forgetting mechanisms to work.

## Key Claims
- Repetition is not neutral; what a person revisits can become easier for the brain to retrieve again.
- The source distinguishes being sad or quiet from actively replaying the painful content.
- New, engaging experiences may give the brain other material to consolidate, reducing the dominance of the older painful trace.
- Running, movies, games, new books, or small pleasant changes are framed as interruption and replacement tools, not as denial.
- The episode's 24-hour framing is a practical popular-science rule of thumb, not a clinical protocol for trauma.
- Complex or severe trauma still needs care, pacing, relationship safety, and professional boundaries beyond ordinary distraction advice.
