# Drowning Forensic Uncertainty

Drowning forensic uncertainty is the source's warning that drowning cannot be diagnosed from one dramatic sign. [[121-jijian-fayi-jianshi-shouce-yiqie-fanzui-jieyou-henji-775064343]] contrasts crime-fiction tropes with the book's account: water in the lungs, lack of water in the lungs, foam, and immersion history all require cautious interpretation.

The concept is a case study in [[ObservationBeforeInference]]. A body may take in water after death, some drowning deaths may leave dry lungs, and older signs may fall out of use when evidence does not support them. The episode uses drowning to teach why [[ForensicScience|forensic science]] often works by excluding alternatives rather than reading one decisive clue.

## Key Claims
- A single bodily sign is rarely enough to determine drowning.
- Postmortem water exposure can imitate or obscure signs.
- Historical forensic rules can be abandoned when evidence weakens them.
- Drowning analysis needs scene context, autopsy findings, toxicology, witness evidence, and competing hypotheses.
