# Sustainable Wild Harvesting

Sustainable wild harvesting is the conservation-economics argument that a bounded harvest from a resilient wild population can protect habitat when it gives local people income from keeping that habitat intact. [[the-little-pet-fish-that-saved-a-town-in-the-amazon]] adds the concept through [[CardinalTetra]] collection around [[Barcelos]] on the [[RioNegro]].

The source does not treat all extraction as sustainable. Its argument depends on a specific biological claim: Cardinal Tetras were found to be extremely abundant, and many die naturally as water levels fall during the dry season. Under those conditions, the episode says wild collection could support the [[PiaberoEconomy]] while reducing pressure for more destructive land use.

## Key Claims
- Harvest sustainability depends on population dynamics, seasonality, and actual removal pressure rather than on whether the product is wild by definition.
- Local livelihood can become a conservation tool when income depends on intact habitat.
- The tradeoff must remain evidence-bound; overharvesting concerns should be tested rather than waved away by cultural attachment.
- Sustainability is not enough by itself if farmed competitors destroy the market that funds local habitat protection.
- The concept therefore depends on economics, not just ecology: buyers must still value the wild product.
