# Snake Religious Mythology

Snake religious mythology is the cross-cultural pattern in [[128-xianliao-shier-shengxiao-zhi-she-cong-yemengjiade-dao-zhaolinger-797116305]] where [[Snake|蛇]] appears near creation, world boundaries, cosmic danger, divine support, ritual authority, and emotional discipline. The episode compares Chinese Fuxi-Nuwa material, Eden's serpent, Jormungandr, Indian world snakes, Vasuki, Apep, Kukulkan, Buddhist naga, and Japanese snake beings.

The concept is not a claim that all serpent traditions share one origin. It is a comparison frame: cultures repeatedly use snakes to imagine forces that surround the world, renew life, threaten order, guard thresholds, or hold knowledge that humans want but cannot fully command.

## Key Claims
- Snakes become religiously potent because their real form already feels liminal: earthbound but elusive, vulnerable but dangerous, deathly but renewal-coded.
- Serpent myths often bind destruction and restoration together.
- Buddhist naga translation into dragon-king language shows [[ChineseFolkReligionLayering]] at work: snake, dragon, deity, and local water authority can overlap.
- Similar serpent motifs need [[MythicSourceLayering]] and [[StoryMotifTransmission]] caution before being treated as direct borrowing.
