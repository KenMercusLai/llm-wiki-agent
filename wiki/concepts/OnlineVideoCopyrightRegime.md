# Online Video Copyright Regime

Online video copyright regime names the regulatory, legal, and commercial shift that moved Chinese video platforms away from pirated or loosely uploaded professional content. In [[no-204-hulianwang-shipin-pingtai-hunzhan-cong-houshe-nansheng-dao-manchangde-jijie-zhongguo-hulianwang-gushi-21-980902194]], the 2008 online-audiovisual rules, lawsuits, and rising drama/variety prices become the dividing line between the early web-video era and the long-video capital war.

The concept matters because it changed what "good video platform" meant. Bandwidth and playback speed still mattered, but legal access to desirable content became expensive, exclusive, and strategic, pushing [[Youku]], [[Tudou]], [[IQIYI]], [[TencentVideo]], and [[SohuVideo]] into rights buying and self-production.

## Key Claims
- Copyright enforcement raised the cost of competing in long video.
- Exclusive rights converted content from commodity inventory into strategic differentiation.
- The regime favored platforms with financing, parent-company backing, or owned content pipelines.
- It weakened independent platforms whose early growth depended on low-cost content supply.
