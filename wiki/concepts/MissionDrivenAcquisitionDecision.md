# Mission-Driven Acquisition Decision

Mission-driven acquisition decision is the source's pattern where a founder weighs selling not only by price, but by whether the buyer can advance the mission better than continued independence. In [[tsr-s4-surbhisarna-v3-tsr-s4-surbhisarna-v3]], [[SurbhiSarna]] says she declined earlier life-changing acquisition interest because she wanted [[NVisionMedical|nVision Medical]] to produce data in patients with cancer.

The later decision changed after the company had FDA clearances, additional clinical data, strategic buyer interest, and a difficult independent commercialization path ahead. Sarna says she came to trust [[BostonScientific]]'s team and culture and had to consider investor outcomes and the challenge of raising a much larger commercialization round.

The concept extends [[PostAcquisitionFounderIdentity]]. The sale was not the end of Sarna's involvement: she stayed with the acquirer, worked on hospital preselling, and then saw COVID disrupt the planned April 2020 launch.

## Key Claims
- An acquisition offer can be emotionally and financially compelling before it is strategically right for the product.
- Mission alignment includes clinical evidence, commercialization capacity, team trust, and whether the acquirer can carry the product into real use.
- Investor responsibility can be part of mission-driven judgment, especially when continued independence would require another high-risk financing round.
- Staying after acquisition can preserve product continuity, but the founder's operating control and external shock exposure change.
