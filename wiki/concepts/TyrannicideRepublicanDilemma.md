# Tyrannicide Republican Dilemma / 弑君式共和困境

Tyrannicide republican dilemma / 弑君式共和困境 is the problem [[124-kaisa-zhisi-lishi-de-yunjiao-shifou-yi-shixiao-781894361]] develops around the [[CaesarAssassination|assassination of Julius Caesar]]. If [[JuliusCaesar|Caesar]] was becoming a king or dictator beyond republican control, killing him could be framed as defense of the [[RomanRepublic|Roman Republic]]. But murder also bypassed law, depended on elite conspiracy, and did not automatically recreate the institutions whose name it invoked.

The source keeps both sides visible. [[MarcusBrutus]] gives the plot a legal and moral self-image by refusing broader purges, while [[GaiusCassiusLonginus]] and other conspirators bring ambition, resentment, fear, and calculation into the same action. The result is an act that could preserve republican memory while failing as republican statecraft.

[[125-mala-zhisi-beibi-de-ansha-huo-zhengyi-de-chujue-783972516]] adds a French Revolution version through [[CharlotteCorday|Charlotte Corday]] and [[JeanPaulMarat|Jean-Paul Marat]]. Corday is not defending an old monarchy in the episode's reading; she is a moderate republican using unlawful violence against a revolutionary figure she believes is driving massacre and terror. The dilemma therefore shifts from elite Roman conspiracy to lone-actor assassination under [[RevolutionaryTerrorPolitics]].

## Key Claims
- Violence against a would-be tyrant can be morally intelligible without being politically sufficient.
- A conspiracy can borrow the language of law while acting outside ordinary legal process.
- Limiting violence can preserve moral meaning but weaken strategic success.
- The public may not read elite tyrannicide the way conspirators expect, especially when the dead ruler had popular benefits and charisma.
- Killing one ruler does not rebuild trust, offices, military loyalty, or succession rules.
- Lone-actor assassination can preserve political refusal without stopping the wider terror system that made the act imaginable.
