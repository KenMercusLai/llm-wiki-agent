# Baseball Reserve Clause

The baseball reserve clause is the contract mechanism in [[how-we-got-free-agents-in-baseball]] that let a [[MajorLeagueBaseball|Major League Baseball]] club keep controlling a player's rights after the contract term. In [[CurtFlood|Curt Flood]]'s case, the clause meant he could play for the [[StLouisCardinals|St. Louis Cardinals]], accept a trade to the [[PhiladelphiaPhillies|Philadelphia Phillies]], or quit baseball.

The source treats the reserve clause as a practical [[LaborMonopsony|labor monopsony]] and a dignity problem. It limited competition among employers, suppressed player bargaining power, and made assignment to a city or team a club decision rather than a player choice.

## Key Claims
- The reserve clause restricted player exit, not just salary negotiation.
- The clause was defended as necessary for [[SportsCompetitiveBalance|competitive balance]].
- Flood's challenge failed in court but helped make the clause publicly and politically vulnerable.
- The later arbitration turn depended on reading "the period of one year" as one renewal year rather than indefinite renewals.
