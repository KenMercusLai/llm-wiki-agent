# Forgetting As Cognitive Function / 遗忘作为认知功能

Forgetting as cognitive function is the episode's core reframing of bad-memory anxiety. In [[143-yiwang-de-naoli-jixing-buhao-ke-zha-zheng-a-869667634]], [[MihuanChishu|蜜獾吃书]] uses [[YiwangDeNaoli|《遗忘的脑力》]] to argue that the brain is not failing when it forgets ordinary material; it is filtering, conserving energy, protecting against overload, and making room for more useful patterns.

The concept matters because it separates memory value from total storage. The source treats perfect recall as potentially painful, inefficient, and identity-disrupting, while ordinary forgetting can help knowledge become gist, painful experience become less gripping, and reading become a change in mood or judgment even after details disappear.

## Key Claims
- Memory is selective and constructive, not a neutral recording of everything that entered the senses.
- Forgetting can be passive decay, but the episode also presents active biological cleanup and pruning as part of a healthy system.
- Emotional intensity makes some memories harder to forget, so "important" to the brain does not always mean useful to the person.
- Remembering better still depends on forgetting: limited capacity forces the brain to compress, abstract, and discard.
- Aging-related forgetting is not treated only as loss; the source argues that older brains may also clear irrelevant new details because life experience already supplies many patterns.
- Forgetting a book or conversation does not prove it had no effect; implicit traces can still shape taste, feeling, language, and later judgment.
- The source is a popular-science account, so mechanism claims should be treated as episode-grounded synthesis rather than a substitute for neuroscience review or clinical advice.
