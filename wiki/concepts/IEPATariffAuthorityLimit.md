# IEPA Tariff Authority Limit

IEPA tariff authority limit is the source's lesson from the Supreme Court ruling in [[the-supreme-court-struck-down-a-bunch-of-trumps-tariffs-now-what]]. The episode says the Trump administration used the International Emergency Economic Powers Act to impose sweeping tariffs, but the [[SupremeCourt]] concluded that a power to regulate importation did not clearly authorize broad tariffs across countries and products.

The concept narrows [[ExecutivePowerPrecedent]] without ending tariff politics. The ruling rejects IEPA as a shortcut for across-the-board tariffs, yet the source emphasizes that Section 301, Section 232, Section 201, and [[Section122TariffAuthority]] still leave tariff tools available with more limits, process, or time constraints.

## Key Claims
- Statutory language matters: "regulate" importation was not enough to authorize sweeping tariffs in the source's account of the ruling.
- The ruling converts [[EffectiveTariffRateShock]] from a pending legal-authority question into a refund and unwind problem.
- The limit applies to the IEPA route, not to every tariff law.
- The case strengthens the distinction between tariff politics as policy preference and tariff authority as delegated legal power.
