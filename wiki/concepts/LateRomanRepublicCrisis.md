# Late Roman Republic Crisis / 罗马共和国晚期危机

Late Roman Republic crisis / 罗马共和国晚期危机 is the structural frame [[124-kaisa-zhisi-lishi-de-yunjiao-shifou-yi-shixiao-781894361]] uses to avoid explaining [[JuliusCaesar|Caesar]]'s rise only through personal ambition. The source says the [[RomanRepublic|Roman Republic]] was already under severe pressure from civil conflict, elite rivalry, popular mobilization, provincial wealth, slave revolt, and the earlier violence of [[GaiusMarius]] and [[LuciusCorneliusSulla]].

The concept matters because it changes the moral question. If the republic were healthy, Caesar could be read mostly as its destroyer. If the republic was already damaged, then Caesar's dictatorship and the [[CaesarAssassination|assassination]] become symptoms of a broader system unable to absorb military command, inequality, patronage, and legitimacy pressure.

## Key Claims
- Caesar is central to the collapse story, but not its only cause.
- Republican institutions can remain formally intact while losing the norms and incentives that make them work.
- Elite republicanism and popular politics are both ambiguous in the source; neither side is treated as pure democracy or pure oligarchy.
- Civil war, death lists, command loyalty, and provincial wealth changed what Roman politics could do.
- The assassination could not restore the republic because it did not repair the underlying crisis.
