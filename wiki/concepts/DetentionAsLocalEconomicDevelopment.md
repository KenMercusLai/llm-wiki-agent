# Detention As Local Economic Development

Detention as local economic development is the pattern where a town treats a detention facility as a source of jobs, public revenue, benefits, and economic stability. In [[the-ice-hiring-boom]], [[FolkstonGeorgia|Folkston, Georgia]] becomes the case: the expanded ICE facility adds about 200 jobs and sends money to the county and city.

The concept is not presented as simple support for detention. [[GlennHall]] emphasizes public-service needs, while [[SavannahPollock]] argues that a community should not rely on a system that harms detained people and falsely frames them as criminals.

## Key Claims
- Detention facilities can look attractive to towns with weak job markets and limited public revenue.
- Benefits such as insurance can make facility jobs especially important for households.
- Economic need can narrow the space for moral debate by making abolition or closure feel like a livelihood threat.
- The development model is fragile when it depends on federal immigration priorities.
