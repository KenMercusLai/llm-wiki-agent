# Black Hair-Care Market Ownership

Black hair-care market ownership is the question of who recognizes, serves, profits from, and controls products made for Black hair and Black consumers. In [[how-black-hair-care-grew-black-power]], [[JohnsonProducts]] built a major business by paying attention to Black customers when many mainstream companies ignored the category, then helped make that category more visible through [[AfroSheen]], [[SoulTrain]], and a mostly Black workforce.

The concept is not just market share. The episode links ownership to product safety and reliability, beauty norms, civil-rights finance, public visibility, community employment, and whether the people whose bodies and culture create demand also capture the value created by that demand.

## Key Claims
- Black hair care can be a major consumer-products category even when mainstream firms initially ignore or misunderstand it.
- Ownership matters because product design, marketing language, hiring, community lending, and cultural sponsorship can all change when the company is embedded in the customer community.
- When a Black-owned company proves the market, larger incumbents can enter with more resources, turning recognition into competition over category value.
- The modern ownership question remains unresolved when products aimed at Black consumers are founded, controlled, or acquired by non-Black owners.
