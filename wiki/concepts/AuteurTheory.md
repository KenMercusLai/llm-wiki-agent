# Auteur Theory

Auteur theory appears in [[108-zu-yu-zhan-ai-yu-bu-ai-bali-jiu-zai-nali-songjian-745812905]] as part of the [[FrenchNewWave|French New Wave]] context around [[FrancoisTruffaut|Francois Truffaut]]. The episode presents the New Wave as a challenge to older film storytelling and as a claim that cinema can bear authorial signature.

In the [[JulesAndJim|《祖与占》]] case, auteur theory does not mean ignoring [[HenriPierreRoche|Henri-Pierre Roche]]'s source novel. It means reading the adaptation as a shaped cinematic work: Truffaut takes Roche's compressed, autobiographical prose and gives it rhythm through voiceover, movement, faces, archival war images, songs, and abrupt tonal shifts.

[[94-jibuli-de-tiancaimen-wo-de-rensheng-jiaogei-reai-he-youyi-722350232]] adds an animation-studio test case. [[HayaoMiyazaki|宫崎骏]] and [[IsaoTakahata|高畑勋]] have unmistakable authorial signatures, but the source shows those signatures becoming films only through [[ToshioSuzuki|铃木敏夫]]'s mediation, [[StudioGhibli|Ghibli]]'s labor system, release pressure, contracts, and publicity. The result is not anti-auteur; it is auteur reading with production conditions kept visible.

## Key Claims
- A film adaptation can respect a source text while still becoming a director-shaped work.
- Auteur attention should not collapse into pure director worship; it should track form, choices, and collaborators.
- [[MoralSuspensionInArtReading]] is especially important when authorial style makes morally unstable material attractive or moving.
- Animation authorship is especially dependent on studio ecology because a director's style must pass through many workers, schedules, and technical decisions.
- Producer mediation can shape the work's possibility and public meaning without erasing the director's authorship.
