# Punishment Theory Tradeoff

Punishment theory tradeoff is the episode's contrast between retribution, utility, and mixed theories of punishment. In [[100-fa-lv-de-bei-lun-xie-tian-xie-di-xing-hao-wo-men-hai-you-luo-xiang-733477302]], [[ImmanuelKant|Kant]] and [[GWFHegel|Hegel]] stand for the idea that punishment recognizes a responsible person, while [[JeremyBentham|Bentham]] stands for punishment as a painful tool for reducing greater social harm.

The source does not accept either pole in absolute form. Retribution without proportion can become vengeance; utility without limits can turn people into warning signs for others. The episode's preference is a constrained comprehensive view that keeps responsibility, deterrence, proportionality, dignity, and [[CriminalLawAsPowerLimitation]] in tension.

[[sp-04-liuqing-linyao-qiangsha-yibao-jutou-an-beihou-de-sikao-shang-guanyu-mincui-sixing-he-sixing-790230209]] adds the death-penalty version through [[CapitalPunishmentRetributionProblem]]. The source asks why "杀人偿命" should survive as literal retribution when modern law usually translates other injuries into non-identical punishment, and why irreversible execution should be treated as obvious when jurisdiction and charge structure can change the outcome.

## Key Claims
- Retribution can mean responsibility and dignity, not only revenge.
- Utilitarian punishment can explain prevention and deterrence, but it can also justify exemplary harshness.
- Light crimes punished too severely can produce resistance, despair, or escalation rather than order.
- A workable punishment theory needs limits from both moral desert and institutional restraint.
- Capital punishment is the hardest retribution test because it joins intuitive proportionality, irreversible state violence, and the risk of treating offenders as warning objects rather than legal persons.
