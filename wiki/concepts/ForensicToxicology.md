# Forensic Toxicology

Forensic toxicology is the use of chemical and biological evidence to detect poisons, drugs, gases, metabolic disruption, and exposure pathways in legal or death-investigation contexts. [[121-jijian-fayi-jianshi-shouce-yiqie-fanzui-jieyou-henji-775064343]] discusses insulin, carbon monoxide, cyanide, volatile solvents, nitrous oxide, and other inhalable or chemically active hazards.

The source shows two sides of toxicology. In historical cases, toxicology can reveal hidden mechanisms such as insulin-induced hypoglycemia or carbon monoxide's displacement of oxygen transport. In public safety, [[InhalationPoisonPublicSafety]] matters because volatile substances may disperse, leave limited residues, or injure people and animals before investigators can identify a clean cause.

## Key Claims
- Toxicology often has to infer exposure from blood, tissues, organ damage, scene context, and chemical behavior.
- Some poisons are hard to detect because they metabolize, dissipate, or leave indirect injury patterns.
- Gas and inhalation exposure can be lethal outside the enclosed-room scenarios that popular imagination expects.
- Toxicology is strongest when paired with scene investigation, medical findings, witness context, and environmental evidence.
