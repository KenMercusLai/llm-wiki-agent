# Book Laundering

Book laundering is the episode's term for disguising ranking-oriented book purchases by spreading them across stores and order sizes so they look more like organic demand. [[how-to-make-a-book-into-a-bestseller]] presents it as a costly, uncertain, and reputationally risky tactic around the [[NewYorkTimesBestsellerList|New York Times bestseller list]].

The practice sits between [[BulkBookBuying]] and ordinary publicity. Instead of simply buying many books at once, an author or sponsor may hire firms to route purchases through likely reporting stores in patterns intended to avoid bulk-sale detection.

## Key Claims
- Book laundering tries to convert concentrated sponsor demand into apparently dispersed reader demand.
- The tactic can cost tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars without guaranteeing list placement.
- It depends on [[BestsellerListOpacity]] because buyers do not know exactly which stores or patterns matter.
- It is more tempting when bestseller status creates value outside book sales, such as speaking fees or consulting work.
