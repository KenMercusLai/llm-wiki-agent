# Assimilation Capacity

Assimilation capacity is the ability of a society to turn newcomers into participants in work, culture, community, and national identity. In [[the-250-year-experiment-americas-birthday-6a477d9aecd123914320b4ad]], [[RobertGuest]] argues that the [[UnitedStates]] still has unusually strong assimilation capacity because its labor market integrates immigrants better than many European systems.

The concept is not automatic optimism. The same source warns that anti-Muslim rhetoric, deportation talk, raids, and harsh enforcement can damage who is treated as American. Assimilation depends on both receiving institutions and political permission: people can work, build families, and contribute culturally while still being excluded symbolically or legally.

## Key Claims
- Labor-market entry is one of America's strongest integration mechanisms.
- Cultural openness and identity formation can make earlier outsiders part of the national story.
- Exclusionary politics can reduce assimilation capacity even when economic integration remains possible.
- Immigration policy design matters because family preference, economic-migrant barriers, and congressional deadlock shape who can join legally.
