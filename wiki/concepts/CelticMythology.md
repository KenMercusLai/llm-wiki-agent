# Celtic Mythology

Celtic mythology enters the wiki through [[142-kaierte-shenhua-gushi-diyidan-deluyi-hui-mengjian-bianxing-yang-ma-865479403]], where [[MihuanChishu|蜜獾吃书]] treats the Celtic field as a multi-region language and story world rather than a single ethnic or national canon. The episode names Ireland, Wales, Scotland, Brittany, Man, England, and Gaul as overlapping contexts, then uses Irish material as the first major entry point.

The episode's version of [[CelticMythology]] is bright without being simple. It contrasts Celtic stories with more fatalistic Norse/Germanic end-time imagery, emphasizing soul continuity, animal transformation, porous death, feasting, hospitality, fairy-like survival, and time differences around the [[CelticOtherworld]]. It also keeps the historical layers visible: older oral material, Christian manuscript preservation, Romantic revival, and modern fantasy all change what "Celtic" means.

## Key Claims
- Celtic mythology is not one clean canon; it is a cluster of regional, linguistic, oral, manuscript, and later literary layers.
- Irish myth is the source's main first case, especially through [[Danu|达努]], [[TuathaDeDanann|Tuatha De Danann / 达努神族]], [[LeborGabalaErenn|《入侵之书》]], [[TuanMacCairill|图安]], [[FionnMacCumhaill|芬恩]], and [[Ossian|Oisin / Ossian / 鹅相]].
- Christianization did not simply erase older mythology; it often preserved and reframed it through Biblical chronology, saint stories, and manuscript culture.
- Animal transformation and reincarnation make identity less tied to one human lifetime than in many modern story worlds.
- Cross-cultural resemblance to Indian, Greek, Norse, Biblical, and fairy-tale material is useful for orientation, but it needs [[MythicSourceLayering]] and [[StoryMotifTransmission]] caution.
- Modern fantasy inherits Celtic motifs unevenly: atmosphere, names, fairies, otherworlds, druids, and heroic cycles can travel without forming direct one-to-one descent.
