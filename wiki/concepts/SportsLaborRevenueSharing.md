# Sports Labor Revenue Sharing

[[how-we-got-free-agents-in-baseball]] adds the longer professional-sports revenue-shift frame. The source says that before [[BaseballFreeAgency|baseball free agency]], less than a quarter of team revenue went to players, while today's major sports labor systems route around half of revenue to players.

Sports labor revenue sharing is the compensation structure in [[diary-of-a-wnba-negotiator]] where players seek a defined share of league basketball revenue rather than only fixed salary increases. The [[WNBAPlayersAssociation]] made this the central demand because fixed raises could become less valuable if [[WNBA]] revenue grew faster than the salary schedule.

The concept extends [[FatLeagueEconomics]] and [[LeagueStakeholderAlignment]]. A league can become more valuable through [[SportsMediaRights]] and [[SportsEntertainmentFlywheel]], but the labor question is who participates in the upside when athletes help create that demand.

## Key Claims
- Revenue share ties player compensation to business growth rather than only to negotiated fixed raises.
- The source says the players initially proposed 40% of revenue as an anchor number and later held at 20%.
- The league's high maximum-salary offers did not solve the players' structural concern if the overall player share would shrink as revenue rose.
- The final deal described by the episode accepted 20% shared basketball revenue, with the share growing over time.
- Free agency can change the effective revenue split even without a single explicit revenue-share formula, because multiple bidders force more league revenue into salaries.
