# Fieldwork Sexual Violence

Fieldwork sexual violence is the risk pattern [[113-henglieta-yu-nachang-jiang-renleixue-songshang-shenpanxi-de-moushaan-756562827]] names by pairing [[HenriettaSchmerler|Henrietta Schmerler]]'s murder with the later [[LinYuping]] case. The episode frames sexual violence as a fieldwork and power problem, not as a feature of one race, region, class, or civilization.

The concept includes the assault itself and the aftermath: disbelief, character attacks, pressure to preserve access, fear of retaliation, institutional silence, and the high social cost carried by the victim. The episode's sharpest claim is that women researchers can be asked to absorb risks that institutions later redescribe as personal naivete or misconduct.

## Key Claims
- Field access can create dependence on local gatekeepers, informants, transport, translation, and reputation, all of which can intensify vulnerability.
- Sexual violence in fieldwork can happen in remote reservations, urban China, elite offices, or any setting where gender and power become asymmetric.
- Post-assault consequences often include monitoring, rumor, professional damage, and pressure to keep the research or institution undisturbed.
- Anti-racist and anti-colonial caution must be preserved, but it cannot be used to deny sexual violence or leave victims outside protection.
- Naming risk should improve protocols and accountability without turning women researchers into people who are automatically excluded from the field.
