# Everyday Behavioral Economics / 日常行为经济学

Everyday behavioral economics is the applied frame in [[155-meimao-neng-dang-fan-chi-ma-xiang-zhuanqian-gai-zuo-dian-sha-jieju-shi-ying-bimian-shenme-xingwei-jingjixue-siwei-you-shenme-yong-920167210]]. The episode starts from the rational-person assumption, then asks how actual people make economic choices under emotion, reference points, fear of loss, free offers, price stories, and social comparison.

The concept extends [[EconomicWayOfThinking]] by adding psychological failure modes. Economics still helps people see costs, incentives, and tradeoffs, but the source argues that real consumers and investors also need to notice how [[LossAversion]], [[MentalAccounting]], [[PriceElasticity]], and marketing design shape what feels rational.

[[live-anthropic-co-founder-on-ai-and-jobs]] adds [[DarylFairweather]]'s company-facing version. Her segment shows behavioral economics inside [[Amazon]] employee engagement work and consumer-facing price systems, where [[DynamicPricingFairness]] separates allocation efficiency from what people experience as fair.

## Key Claims
- Behavioral economics is useful when textbook rationality fails to describe how people actually react to money, price, risk, and identity.
- The same amount of money can feel different depending on whether it is framed as loss, gain, compensation, discount, budget, or sunk effort.
- Commercial design often works by arranging reference points: buffets, refills, free services, subsidies, and coupons all change what the buyer thinks they are paying.
- Everyday behavioral economics should improve agency, not become cynical proof that all markets are manipulation.
- Corporate behavioral economics can shape work design and pricing systems, not only consumer self-help or investing behavior.
- Dynamic pricing shows why perceived fairness remains a real design constraint even when prices efficiently ration scarce supply.
