# Institutional Overcompliance

Institutional overcompliance is the pattern where a low- or mid-level actor intensifies authority by executing its values more zealously than the immediate situation requires. In [[42-anduo-fengqi-yu-qingping-zhi-mo-616391638]], [[SyrilKarn]] embodies this pattern: his superior wants to classify the incident around [[CassianAndor]] as manageable disorder, but Syril insists on pursuing order, discipline, and imperial legitimacy.

The source's point is that a system can become more oppressive through its strivers, not only through lazy or corrupt officials. The person who wants to prove commitment may expose coercion that the institution would otherwise keep hidden.

[[103-ta-shi-cichuan-yewu-de-guang-ta-shi-hanna-alunte-737939789]] adds a darker historical boundary through [[AdolfEichmann|Adolf Eichmann]] and [[NaziGermany|Nazi Germany]]. The episode's [[BanalityOfEvil]] frame shows that institutional harm does not require every actor to be visibly zealous; routine legalistic performance can still make a murderous system work.

[[133-ibm-yu-nacui-weishenme-putongren-bu-ying-zanmei-jizeizhuyi-815684110]] adds technical and statistical overcompliance through [[JacobusLentz|Jacobus Lentz]] and [[PunchedCardAdministration|punched-card administration]]. The episode suggests that an expert who optimizes categories, records, and retrieval for an occupying power can intensify violence even if the work is described as administrative efficiency rather than ideological rage.

## Key Claims
- Overcompliance can make domination more visible and more brittle.
- A zealous functionary may produce escalation even when superiors prefer quiet containment.
- Institutional loyalty is morally ambiguous because effort and discipline depend on the direction of the institution served.
- The pattern helps explain how ordinary enforcement can create new enemies for the regime.
- Ordinary-seeming administrative compliance can be morally catastrophic when the institution's purpose is criminal.
- Technical or statistical excellence can become overcompliance when it perfects a violent institution's classification and control capacity.
