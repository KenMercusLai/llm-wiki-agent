# Dynamic Pricing Fairness

Dynamic pricing fairness is the tension in [[live-anthropic-co-founder-on-ai-and-jobs]] between efficient price adjustment and what buyers experience as legitimate allocation. [[DarylFairweather]] uses rideshare surge pricing, groceries, and [[Amazon]] as examples of prices changing with demand and supply, then separates shortage prevention from perceived fairness.

The concept extends [[EverydayBehavioralEconomics]] because the technically efficient answer may not be the socially acceptable one. A higher price can allocate scarce supply and attract more sellers or drivers, but people may still prefer wait lists, queues, or envy-free systems when price spikes feel arbitrary or exploitative.

## Key Claims
- Dynamic pricing can reduce shortages by letting people who value access more pay more.
- Dynamic pricing feels different depending on whether supply can actually respond; rideshare surge can invite drivers, while grocery supply may not adjust as quickly.
- Fairness can be an explicit design goal rather than an afterthought to price efficiency.
- Consumer discomfort is not necessarily economic ignorance; it may reflect disagreement about what allocation rule should govern scarcity.
