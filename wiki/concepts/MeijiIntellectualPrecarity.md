# Meiji Intellectual Precarity

Meiji intellectual precarity is [[shaqingqing-x-hanzhao-shichuan-zhuomu-wo-baochi-de-sixiang-doushi-yinwei-queqian-894931314]]'s frame for reading [[IshikawaTakuboku|石川啄木]] as a small intellectual caught between modern aspiration and thin institutional support. Takuboku wanted European culture, foreign languages, literary status, and freedom from provincial limits, but he lacked the credentials, money, family stability, and career foothold that writers such as [[NatsumeSoseki|夏目漱石]] or [[MoriOgai|森鸥外]] could more plausibly draw on.

The concept keeps personal responsibility and structure together. Takuboku was often evasive, selfish, and careless with debts and relationships, but the source also shows how low education, rural origin, exam failure, unstable journalism, low pay, fire, illness, and family obligation narrowed his options.

## Key Claims
- Modernization expands desire faster than it distributes routes for acting on that desire.
- Credentials and class position matter for who gets to be a stable professional writer.
- Precarity can push literary form: Takuboku's durable work appears in short, immediate pieces rather than in the long novels he imagined writing.
- A biographical account should not choose between "bad character" and "bad structure" when the source shows both.
- Literary modernity can be carried by people who do not fit the dominant image of successful modern intellectual life.
