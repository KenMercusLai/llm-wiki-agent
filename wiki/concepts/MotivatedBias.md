# Motivated Bias / 动机偏差

Motivated bias is the distortion that comes from wanting an interpretation to be true. [[106-bie-zai-haipa-chongtu-kuai-lai-xuexi-tanpan-shu-742539645]] lists self-serving attribution, overconfidence, irrational optimism, and superiority illusion as examples that can damage negotiation.

The episode's practical warning is that a party may read the other's kindness as weakness or a communication error as malice. If the relationship is still worth preserving, the remedy is more information exchange, clearer explanation, and repeated asking why rather than assuming intent from a preferred story.

[[123-renxing-shiyan-dangxin-ni-ziji-779479569]] extends motivated bias from negotiation into social perception. The source's capital-punishment, sports, political-reporting, and media examples show that people can rate evidence as stronger when it supports their side, then experience that preference as objectivity rather than desire.

## Key Claims
- Motivated bias can make each side see itself as reasonable and the other side as manipulative.
- In social perception, motivated bias can make hostile interpretation feel like neutral evidence.
- Overconfidence narrows the perceived [[ZoneOfPossibleAgreement]] by making one's assumptions feel like facts.
- Transparency and explanation reduce bias when both sides are still worth engaging.
- [[InterestBasedNegotiation]] works partly by replacing attribution stories with information about actual interests.
