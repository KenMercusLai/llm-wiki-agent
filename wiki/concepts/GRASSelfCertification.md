# GRAS Self-Certification

GRAS self-certification is the food-law pathway where a company can treat an ingredient as generally recognized as safe for food use. [[why-is-there-a-supplement-craze-if-they-dont-even-work]] uses [[NeuroShake]] and [[Prevagen]] to show how this route can matter for supplement sales.

[[the-sneaky-way-companies-get-new-chemicals-into-our-food]] makes the pathway broader and more severe. The episode says [[TaraFlour]] entered food through [[SecretGRAS]], meaning the [[FoodAndDrugAdministration|FDA]] was not notified before consumers ate the ingredient and reported serious illness.

The combined point is not that every GRAS determination is invalid. It is that self-certification can move safety judgment into company-controlled processes, and the public may not know whether a new ingredient was reviewed, self-certified with notice, or used without notice.

## Key Claims
- GRAS self-certification can move safety judgment partly into company-controlled processes.
- In the source's [[Prevagen]] case, an ingredient associated with a memory supplement was introduced through [[NeuroShake]] as food before being used in supplement marketing.
- In the [[TaraFlour]] case, [[SecretGRAS]] meant the FDA learned about the ingredient only after harm reports.
- FDA concern did not prevent the source's reported years of sales, showing how slow or limited oversight can be.
- GRAS is a safety concept, not proof that a supplement produces the advertised wellness benefit.
