# Urban Ecology

Urban ecology is the study of how organisms, habitats, infrastructure, and human activity interact inside cities. In [[ganguan-fangda-shijie-he-renning-liao-guanniao-ziran-yu-ziyou-e583dac2-bad8-4208-8d35-0c3de8594779]], [[RenNing]] uses birdwatching to show that city nature includes parks, wetlands, train stations, convenience-store edges, power-line wasteland, vacant lots, landscaped water, and unmanaged native plants.

The source's key urban-ecology move is to treat birds as indicators rather than decoration. If a species appears, disappears, changes abundance, or returns predictably to a branch, that may signal food, water, landscaping, climate, migration habit, disturbance, or a hidden habitat condition. Managed green spaces can help by concentrating habitat, but scenic landscaping can also damage wetland cycles when it freezes water levels and vegetation into stable visitor-friendly views.

[[65-longwang-zhinu-1931-nian-de-changjiang-hongshui-658100922]] adds a historical floodplain-city case through [[Wuhan]], [[Hankou]], [[Yunmengze]], the [[YangtzeRiver|Yangtze]], and the [[HanRiver|Han River]]. The source shows that urban ecology is also about how wetland conversion, hard infrastructure, dikes, warehouses, electric systems, and dense housing change the path from water to disaster.

[[62-kelakesen-de-nongchang-xiangbudao-ni-shi-zheyang-de-xiaoyang-xiaoen-652404260]] adds a water-cycle caution from the farm episode's flood discussion. The source links urban flooding not only to climate but also to hardened ground that prevents water from soaking into soil, turning built surfaces into an ecological and infrastructure decision.

## Key Claims
- Cities contain many small habitats that become visible only through repeated observation.
- "Central Park effect" describes how limited green space can concentrate birds inside dense built environments.
- Urban wasteland may support native plants and birds better than heavily managed ornamental landscapes.
- Wetland parks can become ecological contradictions when they prioritize stable scenery over dry-wet variation.
- Birds can indicate environmental change because they leave unsuitable places and follow food, climate, and habitat conditions.
- Hard surfaces, drainage, and water absorption make cities part of the same land-management problem as farms and watersheds.
- Wetland loss can turn a floodplain city from an adaptive water landscape into a fixed infrastructure system exposed to catastrophic failure.
- Modern urban hazards include chemicals, oil, electricity, crowding, rent, and shelter design as well as water depth.
