# Distribution Before Monetization

Distribution before monetization is the startup pattern where product value and user access justify financing before a clear revenue model exists. In [[tsr-s4-ronconway-part3-v2-tsr-s4-ronconway-part3-v2]], [[RonConway]] says [[Google]] had no clear revenue model at the time of its [[KleinerPerkins]] and [[SequoiaCapital]] round, but [[LarryPage]] and [[SergeyBrin]] had a strong plan to get distribution through [[AOL]] and [[Yahoo]] while building the Google brand.

The source's resolution is [[AdWords]]. [[PageRankSearchRelevance]] created a product users wanted, distribution deals could increase usage, and [[SalarKamangar]] later helped turn search attention into advertising revenue. The pattern is therefore not "ignore monetization"; it is sequence product quality, distribution, and revenue in the order the market allows.

[[tsr-ronconway-part4-v2-tsr-ronconway-part4-v2]] adds the process pressure behind that sequence. [[RonConway]] says [[LarryPage]] considered replacing the delayed strategic round with a large angel round, but Conway and [[RamShriram]] forced a deadline to preserve [[KleinerPerkins]] and [[SequoiaCapital]] because their [[AOL]] and [[Yahoo]] routes mattered before monetization was solved.

## Key Claims
- A large unsolved user problem can justify investment before revenue if usage and distribution paths are credible.
- Distribution partners can reduce adoption risk while a young company builds direct brand demand.
- Delayed monetization becomes dangerous if the company never finds a mechanism like [[AdWords]] that fits the user behavior.
- The pattern connects to [[DistributionLedProductBuilding]] but is narrower: it describes a stage where distribution is legible before revenue.
- Strategic distribution can justify a harder fundraising process when the investors bring specific market access.
