# Self-Recognition Horror

Self-recognition horror is the pattern in which the frightening other turns out to be an unrecognized version of oneself. In [[130-tuokaerqiuke-guaidan-gushiji-xunzhao-he-gaobie-803543569]], the concept comes from 〈旅客〉 in [[BizarreStories|《怪诞故事集》]]: a child fears a dim face with a glowing red point, and decades later the adult recognizes that figure as his own aging reflection with a cigarette.

The episode reads this not as a cheap twist but as a compressed account of internal life. Childhood terror cannot be soothed by parents because the child lacks language for what he sees. Later recognition brings relief because the monster is not an external invader, yet it also exposes loneliness, mortality, and the disappearance of the guardians who once came running.

## Key Claims
- The horror comes from delayed identity: the feared figure is both self and stranger.
- Age makes selfhood temporal. A child can be haunted by the old person he will become, and the old person can finally understand the child's fear.
- The pattern sits between [[EverydayWeirdness]] and [[EmbodiedAging]] because the supernatural effect depends on ordinary reflection, smoke, skin, eyes, and loneliness.
