# Hindsight Bias / 后见之明偏差

Hindsight bias is the tendency to treat a reported result as obvious after learning it. In [[123-renxing-shiyan-dangxin-ni-ziji-779479569]], the hosts use a 1949-style example where people saw false conclusions about soldiers and still felt the claims were common sense.

The episode uses hindsight bias to defend experimental inquiry. A claim about human nature can feel like something "everyone knows," but the same audience may accept the opposite claim once it is packaged as a result.

## Key Claims
- Common-sense recognition is not proof.
- Hindsight can make weak or false explanations feel inevitable.
- The bias supports self-serving stories because people can retrofit success, failure, or moral judgment after the outcome is known.
- [[ScientificSelfCorrection]] matters because it keeps "obvious" claims answerable to evidence.

## Connections
- [[SocialPsychology]] - discipline whose findings are often mistaken for mere common sense.
- [[MotivatedBias]] - related tendency to protect preferred explanations.
- [[RationalHumility]] - posture needed when an explanation feels too easy.
