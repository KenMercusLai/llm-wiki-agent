# Bestseller List Opacity

Bestseller list opacity is the deliberate secrecy around which stores report sales, how those sales are weighted, and how suspicious sales patterns are handled. [[how-to-make-a-book-into-a-bestseller]] adds the concept through the [[NewYorkTimesBestsellerList|New York Times bestseller list]].

The source presents opacity as a governance tradeoff. Secrecy can reduce direct manipulation, but it also creates distrust, optimization guesses, legal conflict, and a gap between "sold many copies" and "made the list."

## Key Claims
- Hiding reporting stores and weighting can make direct gaming harder.
- Secrecy also rewards inference, rumor, and specialized launch advice.
- [[JacquelineSusann]]'s targeted bookstore strategy shows how authors may act on suspected reporting channels.
- [[WilliamPeterBlatty]]'s lawsuit shows how opacity becomes contentious when a book appears to have sold strongly but is excluded.
- [[PatrickHealy]]'s defense of the Times methodology makes legitimacy and anti-gaming protection part of the same system.
