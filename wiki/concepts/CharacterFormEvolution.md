# Character Form Evolution

Character form evolution is the source's nameable pattern where a Chinese character changes across oracle-bone script, bronze inscriptions, seal script, clerical script, traditional forms, simplified forms, and modern print. In [[57-shuojie-hanzi-shiniaopi-weisha-shi-shi-zitou-642970319]], this is the reason the hosts reject the easy claim that today's characters are simply pictures.

The concept is visible in the episode's examples. 人, 大, 从, 及, 众, 集, 尸, 尿, 屎, 包, 孕, 弃, 鬼, 舞, and 万 all become small case studies in how early images become conventional signs, how components shift, and how later shapes can hide the body posture, labor scene, ritual role, or animal image that first made a character legible.

## Key Claims
- The same early visual source can split into several later meanings and forms.
- Later writing aesthetics and standardization can make an older shape look more abstract.
- Simplified forms are not automatically shallower or later; some may be closer to older forms than the received traditional form.
- Components can preserve posture, action, or category rather than today's most familiar meaning.
- Explaining a form requires sequence, not only resemblance.
