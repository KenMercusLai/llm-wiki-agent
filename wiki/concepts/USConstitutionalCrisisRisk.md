# U.S. Constitutional Crisis Risk

U.S. constitutional crisis risk is [[sp-05-liuqing-linyao-qiangsha-yibao-jutou-an-beihou-de-sikao-xia-fenlie-de-meiguo-hui-you-xianzheng-weiji-ma-790241978]]'s frame for a country that may keep functioning formally while its institutions become less able to repair conflict. [[LinYao|林垚]] does not predict imminent civil war. He argues that the more likely danger is prolonged disorder: contested mandates, brittle constitutional design, party polarization, weakened local reform channels, and uncertainty over what comes after [[DonaldTrump]] and [[MAGA]].

The concept extends [[AmericanDemocraticResilience]] by naming the negative case. Resilience asks whether courts, elections, civil society, and constitutional repair mechanisms can still respond. Constitutional crisis risk asks what happens when those mechanisms continue to exist but no longer create enough trusted settlement.

## Key Claims
- A formal election victory does not automatically settle legitimacy when both sides use "democracy crisis" language with incompatible meanings.
- Crisis can be chronic rather than spectacular: stalemate, executive norm erosion, institutional distrust, and unstable coalition politics may matter more than one dramatic breakdown.
- Civil war is judged less likely because the modern federal state controls the military capacity that nineteenth-century states once could contest.
- [[PresidentialSystem|Presidentialism]] raises risk because fixed executive terms can produce either semi-paralysis or strongman-style action when legislative conflict hardens.
- [[USConstitutionalReformConstraint]] makes ordinary self-correction difficult, so pressure can accumulate outside formal amendment channels.
- Generational replacement may solve some conflicts slowly, but that is a weak substitute for active institutional repair.
