# Cross-Cultural Product Design

Cross-cultural product design is the design-side counterpart to [[CrossCulturalUserResearch]]. In [[ep87-duihua-duli-shejishi-daqi-tongguo-sheji-bangzhu-chanpin-zuohao-zengzhang-luymytt48g-ejwsl6bfuko2xsdoc]], [[Daqi]] uses his [[Lazada]] and [[TikTok]] experience to show that outbound products need local adaptation in interface, copy, trust signals, and formatting rather than only translation.

The episode's concrete examples include color preferences across China, Southeast Asia, and Western markets; green as an accepted brand color in Muslim-majority markets; language expansion that changes layout; and date formats that differ by country. These details matter because small interface assumptions can make a product feel local, foreign, trustworthy, or confusing.

## Key Claims

- Cultural differences may be modest at the level of broad user need but large at the level of design cues, density, color, language, and trust.
- Localization is not a final copy pass; it can affect layout, component sizing, information order, and conversion behavior.
- Real user visits and account-manager knowledge can turn abstract personas into concrete market understanding.
- Product teams should distinguish global product logic from local expression: the same problem may need different proof, copy, or visual weight in different markets.
