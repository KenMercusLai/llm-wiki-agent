# Creator Responsibility Ethics / 创造者责任伦理

Creator responsibility ethics is the source's main moral frame for [[Frankenstein|《弗兰肯斯坦》]]. In [[141-zhizao-fulankensitan-kehuan-zhimu-he-tade-shenqi-shidai-857923931]], Victor Frankenstein's central failure is not simply that he creates life, but that he abandons what he creates and refuses the ongoing responsibilities created by his own act.

The concept separates scientific ambition from care. A maker may be thrilled by technical possibility, but the created being still needs recognition, education, companionship, boundaries, and accountability. That is why the source links scientific imagination to [[CreatureSocialExclusion]] rather than reducing the novel to a warning against knowledge itself.

## Key Claims
- Making something alive or agentic creates obligations that do not end at technical success.
- Abandonment can convert a creation problem into a social and moral disaster.
- The source treats responsibility as relational: the maker owes care, but the surrounding society also shapes what the creature becomes.
