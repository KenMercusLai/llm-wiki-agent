# Superstar Cities

Superstar cities are economically successful metros where high-opportunity jobs have increasingly concentrated. [[why-are-we-so-obsessed-with-manufacturing]] uses the label to explain why manufacturing retains political appeal: many towns lost factories and hope new production could spread opportunity beyond large urban winners.

The concept is not anti-city. It names a distribution problem in opportunity, where high-value sectors cluster in a limited number of places while other regions lose employers, supplier networks, and local spending ecosystems.

## Key Claims
- Job opportunities have become more concentrated in big cities over the last 50 years, according to the episode.
- Manufacturing is politically appealing partly because it may locate where media, finance, and tech do not.
- A factory revival is only one possible answer; [[TradableSectorLocalGrowth]] can also come from colleges, hospitals, agriculture, tech, mining, tourism, or other export-base sectors.
- [[ManufacturingRegionalMultiplier]] matters because regional policy cares about local spillovers, not only direct jobs.
