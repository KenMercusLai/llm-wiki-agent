# Maritime Chokepoint Tolling

Maritime chokepoint tolling is the use of control over a narrow shipping passage to charge, screen, delay, or condition vessel movement. [[how-to-get-through-the-strait-of-hormuz]] introduces the concept through [[HamidHosseini]]'s account that an Indian oil ship requested [[SepahNavy]] permission to pass through the [[StraitOfHormuz]] and paid an alleged one-dollar-per-barrel crypto toll.

The concept is distinct from ordinary port fees because the toll is tied to coercive control during conflict. The episode treats the immediate fee as only one layer: the larger effect is on [[ChokepointShippingConfidence]], insurance, routing, sanctions, and the credibility of the [[FreedomOfNavigationTradeOrder]].

## Key Claims
- A chokepoint toll can convert physical control of a waterway into revenue and bargaining leverage.
- Screening can make passage conditional on a ship's flag, destination, cargo, crew, and adversary links.
- The direct cost may be small relative to cargo value, but the uncertainty can raise shipping, insurance, and inventory risk across markets.
- If tolling replaces free passage, companies may treat long-distance trade routes as politically administered rather than routine infrastructure.
