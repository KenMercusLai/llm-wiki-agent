# Public Space Routine Tracking

Public space routine tracking is the privacy risk that ordinary activity visible in public becomes sensitive when it is recorded, archived, searchable, and reviewed over time. In [[tech-20260108-0108-mp-tech-pod-128-tech-20260108-0108-mp-tech-pod-128]], [[BenJordan]] says that a camera on a running trail feels very different when someone can review 30 days of footage and infer when a person runs alone.

The concept matters because public visibility is not the same as permanent, remote, high-resolution observability. The episode's examples include running schedules, people loading expensive items into vehicles, camera views near homes, phone activity visible through zoomed footage, and cameras pointed at playgrounds.

## Key Claims
- Public behavior can become private-risk information when repeated patterns are easy to reconstruct.
- Archives make surveillance more powerful than a single live glimpse.
- Zoom and image quality can expose details such as phone behavior or license plates that bystanders do not expect to share globally.
- Monitoring can change behavior even before footage is misused, which links the concept to [[CivilLibertiesSurveillanceRisk]].
- Public-space routine tracking becomes more dangerous when paired with [[CrossDatasetPrivacyLinkage]].
