# Chinese Zodiac / 十二生肖

Chinese Zodiac / 十二生肖 is the animal-cycle frame shared by [[127-xianliao-shier-shengxiao-zhi-long-shenqi-dongwu-zai-nali-788239655]] and [[171-xianliao-shier-shengxiao-zhi-ma-guanyin-dashi-de-xingqu-aihao-ji-laotouhuan-juese-de-linggan-959614962]]. In the dragon episode, [[MihuanChishu|蜜獾吃书]] introduces the zodiac as a way to attach concrete animal images to abstract earthly branches, making the timekeeping system easier to remember.

The episode keeps the origin problem open. It mentions Southeast Asian, northern nomadic, Indian Buddhist, and Babylonian theories, then uses Shuihudi Qin-slip material to show that early Chinese branch-animal associations already existed, though the correspondences did not yet match the modern zodiac exactly.

The horse episode adds the parallel later branch through [[Horse|马]] and [[HorseCulturalSymbolism]], while the dragon episode makes [[ChineseDragon|龙]] a more explicit case of symbolic invention. Together they make the zodiac less a fixed list than a recurring prompt for animal history, religion, folklore, language, and modern identity.

## Key Claims
- Zodiac animals make abstract cyclical time more memorable and narratable.
- The origin question should remain evidence-bounded; similarity across regions does not automatically prove one source.
- Early Chinese evidence can show a zodiac-like system without proving the modern list was already fixed.
- Each animal can become a route into a different cultural problem: horse into infrastructure and religious symbolism, dragon into national-symbol invention.
