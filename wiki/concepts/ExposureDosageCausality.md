# Exposure-Dosage Causality

Exposure-dosage causality is the research logic of testing whether more time in a treatment environment produces larger effects. [[can-transforming-neighborhoods-help-kids-escape-poverty]] uses it to explain how [[RajChetty]] and colleagues evaluate [[HOPEVIProgram|HOPE VI]] despite selection-bias concerns.

The source presents two dosage patterns. First, each additional year a child spent in revitalized HOPE VI housing was associated with higher later earnings. Second, sibling comparisons showed younger siblings with more exposure later doing better than older siblings from the same families.

## Key Claims
- Dosage patterns help distinguish treatment effects from simple differences between families.
- Sibling comparisons are valuable because they hold more family background constant.
- Exposure timing matters more for children than adults when a policy operates through development, peers, school, or aspirations.
- The method strengthens but does not by itself prove every possible mechanism.
