# Tax Treaty Arbitrage

Tax treaty arbitrage is the use of mismatches, ambiguities, or reciprocal promises across tax systems to reduce tax liability. [[the-leaked-tapes-that-show-how-the-rich-avoid-taxes]] adds the concept through the [[USMaltaTaxTreaty]], where retirement-account reciprocity between the [[UnitedStates]] and [[Malta]] became the foundation for the [[MaltaTaxLoophole]].

The concept is narrower than all international tax planning. In the source, the arbitrage depends on a specific treaty interaction: U.S. tax law restricts Roth IRA contributions and asset placement, while Maltese retirement accounts were presented as more permissive. Promoters could then argue that treaty treatment transformed a local account rule into a U.S. tax shelter.

## Key Claims
- Treaty language designed to prevent double taxation can create unexpected planning opportunities.
- Differences in account rules, contribution limits, and asset eligibility can become economically important when recognized across borders.
- Clarifying treaty interpretation is one way governments try to close arbitrage without rewriting every domestic rule.
- Tax treaty arbitrage can trigger [[TaxAvoidanceEvasionBoundary|legality fights]] when advisers and agencies disagree about whether the structure respects the treaty's purpose.
