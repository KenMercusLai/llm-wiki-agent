# Free Love Practice Gap / 自由恋爱实践落差

Free Love Practice Gap / 自由恋爱实践落差 is the gap between professed romantic or sexual freedom and the actual distribution of care, jealousy, secrecy, reputation risk, and power inside relationships. [[135-xianliao-weida-zuojiamen-de-bagua-di-san-dan-823746670]] develops the pattern through [[HGWells|H. G. Wells / H. G. 威尔斯]], [[JeanPaulSartre|Jean-Paul Sartre / 萨特]], and [[AynRand|Ayn Rand / 安·兰德]].

The Wells section makes the gap gendered and practical. The episode says Wells spoke and wrote about sexual freedom while relationships such as [[AmberReeves|Amber Reeves / 安伯·里夫斯]]'s pregnancy exposed his concern with scandal, domestic service expectations, and unequal consequences. The [[MuraBudberg|Moura Budberg / 穆拉·巴德伯格]] story then adds a political-intelligence dimension to intimate entanglement.

The Sartre and Rand sections show two different failures of doctrine. Sartre and [[SimoneDeBeauvoir|Simone de Beauvoir / 西蒙娜·德·波伏娃]]'s open relationship becomes part of the public intellectual myth, but the episode complicates it through later lovers and inheritance. Rand's romance-freedom doctrine is tested by [[NathanielBranden|Nathaniel Branden / 纳撒尼尔·布兰登]], where the source sees a double standard between principle and jealousy.

## Key Claims
- Sexual or romantic liberation rhetoric can still preserve unequal power.
- The real test of a freedom doctrine is how it handles pregnancy, domestic labor, jealousy, reputation, and replacement.
- Public intellectual myths often make open relationships look cleaner than the lived arrangements were.
- Relationship gossip needs source caution, but repeated contradictions can still illuminate doctrine-practice gaps.
