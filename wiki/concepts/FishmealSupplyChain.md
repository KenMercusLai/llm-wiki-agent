# Fishmeal Supply Chain

Fishmeal supply chain is the hidden commodity route described in [[shangye-xiaoyang-45-eerninuo-yueda-yu-yuegui-1000164352]]: [[PeruvianAnchoveta|anchoveta]] caught off [[Peru]] is processed into protein-rich fishmeal and fish oil, then blended into aquaculture feed for salmon, shrimp, sea bass, and other farmed seafood. The source treats fishmeal as a small, obscure input that can still move visible consumer prices when supply tightens.

The concept connects ecology, commodity concentration, feed formulation, and restaurant supply. [[ElNino]] can reduce anchoveta populations; [[FisheriesResourceManagement]] can restrict catches to protect future stock; and [[AquacultureFeedDependency]] means high-value farmed seafood still needs some marine-origin nutrition even when soy, rapeseed meal, pea protein, and other substitutes reduce inclusion rates.

## Key Claims
- Small biological inputs can become global commodity constraints when they sit upstream of large consumer categories.
- Supply concentration matters: the episode treats [[Peru]], [[Chile]], and [[Ecuador]] as a regional cluster whose disruptions can affect global fishmeal pricing.
- Substitution changes the slope of dependency but does not eliminate it when animal nutrition, growth, and Omega-3 requirements remain binding.
- Fishmeal shocks can transmit through farm output, restaurant procurement, and final menu prices rather than appearing directly to consumers.
- Long-term stock protection can worsen short-term scarcity while still being rational for future supply.
