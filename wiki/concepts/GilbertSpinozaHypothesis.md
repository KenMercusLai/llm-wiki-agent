# Gilbert-Spinoza Hypothesis / 吉尔伯特-斯宾诺莎假设

The Gilbert-Spinoza hypothesis is the episode's frame for belief-first cognition: people initially accept information and then need later cognitive effort to reject it. [[123-renxing-shiyan-dangxin-ni-ziji-779479569]] attributes the modern psychology formulation to [[DanielGilbert]] and traces the older philosophical contrast to [[BaruchSpinoza|Baruch Spinoza / 斯宾诺莎]].

The source uses the hypothesis to explain why false statements can still influence judgment when people are distracted, hurried, or surrounded by noisy presentation. A person may know a marked claim is false and still have their sentencing judgment, political impression, or scam susceptibility shifted by it.

## Key Claims
- Belief can be the fast default, while disbelief requires effort.
- Distraction weakens rejection more than initial acceptance.
- False information can leave residue even after the person explicitly knows it is false.
- Fast cuts, loud presentation, urgent timing, and sensory clutter can therefore be persuasion tools.
