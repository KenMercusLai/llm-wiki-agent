# Restaurant Automation

Restaurant automation is the use of machines or software to take over, standardize, or assist restaurant tasks such as cooking, timing, frying, saucing, cleaning, ordering, and staffing. In [[chef-vs-robot]], [[RobbyWokbot]] at [[Instafoods]] makes the concept concrete by automating parts of wok cooking while still requiring [[KennyPoon]] to add ingredients.

The source treats restaurant automation as a labor and operating-margin problem. Restaurants are labor-intensive and often run on thin margins, so a robot becomes attractive when it lowers training time, increases throughput, improves consistency, or lets owners hire less specialized workers. The tradeoff is that some food quality depends on skill, sensory judgment, and heat techniques such as [[WokHei]].

## Key Claims
- Automation may arrive first in repetitive or bounded food tasks where diners value speed, consistency, price, or availability more than craft nuance.
- Partial automation can change skill requirements without eliminating all labor.
- Restaurant robots can reduce training and staffing pressure but add breakdown and maintenance risk.
- Taste differences matter most where the dish depends on technique, high heat, and subtle sensory control.
- Restaurant automation should be analyzed through [[AutomationDisplacementEffect]] and [[AutomationReinstatementEffect]] rather than assumed to destroy or create jobs uniformly.
