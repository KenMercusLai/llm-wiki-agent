# Baseball Free Agency

Baseball free agency is the labor-market outcome in [[how-we-got-free-agents-in-baseball]] that followed [[CurtFlood|Curt Flood]]'s failed legal challenge, later arbitration, and [[SportsCollectiveBargaining|collective bargaining]]. The source says players tested the [[BaseballReserveClause|reserve clause]] language by playing one year without signing new contracts and arguing that renewal lasted only "the period of one year."

The arbitrator ruled for the players, letting them sign with willing teams after that year. The episode presents free agency as a shift from club assignment toward [[SportsPlayerAutonomy|player autonomy]], higher pay, and a larger player share of sports revenue.

## Key Claims
- Free agency changed the market from one controlled buyer to multiple potential bidders.
- The source says baseball's eventual system allowed players to become free agents after their sixth year in the major leagues.
- The same shift helped move sports economics toward players receiving around half of revenue.
- Free agency leaves a [[SportsCompetitiveBalance|competitive-balance]] debate because richer teams can bid more aggressively.
