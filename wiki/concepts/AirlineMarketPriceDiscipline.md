# Airline Market Price Discipline

Airline market price discipline is the broader market effect attributed to [[SpiritAirlines|Spirit Airlines]] in [[spirit-airlines-and-the-future-of-cheap-flights]]. Even passengers who dislike flying Spirit may benefit if Spirit's low fares force larger airlines to offer cheaper options or keep some routes more price-competitive.

The concept separates consumer satisfaction from competitive value. A carrier can have disliked service and still discipline market prices if rivals have to respond to its fares. The episode's concern is that Spirit's disappearance could reduce that pressure on [[DeltaAirLines|Delta Air Lines]], [[AmericanAirlines|American Airlines]], [[UnitedAirlines|United Airlines]], and other incumbents.

## Key Claims
- A low-cost entrant can benefit non-customers by forcing incumbents to lower or segment fares.
- Satisfaction scores do not measure the full competitive role of a firm.
- Merger review and bailout debate can both turn on whether the firm disciplines market prices.
- If low-fare pressure disappears, customers who never chose the budget carrier may still pay more.
