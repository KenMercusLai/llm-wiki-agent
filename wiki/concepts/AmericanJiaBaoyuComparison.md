# American Jia Baoyu Comparison

American Jia Baoyu comparison is the source's cross-cultural reading of [[HoldenCaulfield|Holden Caulfield / 霍尔顿]] from [[TheCatcherInTheRye|《麦田里的守望者》]] through [[JiaBaoyu|贾宝玉]] from [[HongLouMeng|《红楼梦》]]. In [[01-maitian-shouwangzhe-20shiji-de-meiguo-jiabaoyu-523554422]], the hosts call Holden a "20th-century American Jia Baoyu" to make his temperament legible to Chinese readers.

The comparison rests on class, age, sensibility, and refusal. Both figures are teenage or near-teenage privileged boys who are expected to enter the adult order through study, status, family duty, and masculine usefulness. Both are drawn to literature and emotional intimacy, especially with girls or younger female relatives, and both are disgusted by vulgarity, hypocrisy, coarseness, and social performance.

The source also keeps cultural difference visible. [[JiaBaoyu|Baoyu]] stands inside Confucian family order and late-imperial Chinese social hierarchy, while [[HoldenCaulfield|Holden]] stands inside postwar American modernity, school discipline, consumer culture, Hollywood falseness, and religious or moral dislocation. The comparison therefore does not claim identity; it uses analogy to show how different societies can produce a similar figure of sensitive youth resisting adult capture.

## Key Claims
- Comparative reading can make a foreign literary character quickly legible without erasing local historical difference.
- Holden and Baoyu are both anti-utilitarian youth figures: their resistance is not only laziness, but a refusal to let feeling be absorbed into status and adult performance.
- The comparison works best when it stays with temperament, class position, sibling tenderness, schooling, vulgarity, and adult-world disgust rather than pretending the novels share one plot or social system.
- The analogy extends [[ClassicReadingComplexity]] because it reopens both works through reader memory rather than reducing either to a school label.
