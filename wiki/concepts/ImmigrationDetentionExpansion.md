# Immigration Detention Expansion

Immigration detention expansion is the rapid increase in detention capacity described in [[the-ice-hiring-boom]]. The source says about 71,000 people were in detention, a record high, and that the administration wanted capacity for about 93,000 people at the same time while planning more than $38 billion in detention spending.

The concept links enforcement personnel to physical infrastructure. More arrests require beds, facilities, contracts, local labor, and political agreements, which means immigration enforcement becomes a development and procurement story as well as a border-control story.

## Key Claims
- Detention capacity is an enabling constraint for mass enforcement policy.
- Expansion shifts federal immigration policy into small towns that can supply land, labor, and political support.
- Private operators can turn detention growth into contract revenue.
- Capacity goals can create local dependencies that persist even if immigration policy later changes.
