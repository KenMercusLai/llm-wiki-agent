# Forensic Death-Time Inference

Forensic death-time inference is the attempt to estimate when death occurred from body condition, environment, stomach contents, insects, freezing, burial, water exposure, and other traces. [[121-jijian-fayi-jianshi-shouce-yiqie-fanzui-jieyou-henji-775064343]] repeatedly warns that fictional precision is misleading: real estimates often need ranges and multiple evidence types.

The episode's examples include stomach contents matched to a last meal, frozen bodies that rot from the outside inward after thawing, decomposition studies from [[BodyFarmForensicResearch]], and insect life cycles captured by [[ForensicEntomology]]. These methods can narrow possibilities, but diet, temperature, season, access by insects, and environmental disturbance all affect timing.

## Key Claims
- Death-time estimates are probabilistic and environment-dependent.
- Stomach contents can support identity or sequence claims, but they should not be treated as a precise clock.
- Decomposition research improves estimates while demonstrating the limits of universal rules.
- Insects can provide useful stage evidence, but weather, night, season, and cold can change insect behavior.
- The strongest estimates combine several independent traces rather than rely on one neat clue.
