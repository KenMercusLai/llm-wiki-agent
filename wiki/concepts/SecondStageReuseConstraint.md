# Second-Stage Reuse Constraint

Second-stage reuse constraint is [[AndyLapsa]]'s source frame for why partial rocket reuse does not fully solve launch economics. In [[tsr-ycoffsite-andylapsa-v1-audio-tsr-ycoffsite-andylapsa-v1-audio]], he says [[SpaceX]] changed the industry by reusing the first stage, but that current partially reusable systems still throw away the second stage on every flight.

The constraint is both economic and operational. Economically, the upper stage must be rebuilt each time, so its structure, engines, components, certification, and testing remain part of marginal launch cost. Operationally, flight rate depends on how quickly those second stages and their checks can be produced, which means upper-stage production can limit launch cadence even when first-stage boosters are available.

This concept narrows [[ReusableRocketEconomics]] from the general value of reuse to the specific remaining bottleneck that [[Stoke|Stoke Space]] is trying to attack through full reusability. It also explains why Andy treats [[Starship]] as the relevant benchmark: it is intended to be fully reusable, but the source says it had not yet achieved that state at the time of the episode.

## Key Claims
- First-stage reuse can lower costs and increase launch rate, but discarded second stages preserve a large per-flight production burden.
- The upper stage matters to both marginal cost and capacity, because every flight still needs a newly built and tested stage.
- Full reusability changes the production problem from building major flight hardware for every mission toward inspecting, maintaining, and turning around already-built vehicles.
- The second-stage bottleneck helps explain why launch has not yet reached ordinary transportation cadence even after [[Falcon9]] reuse.
