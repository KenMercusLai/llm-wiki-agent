# Liberalism-Paternalism Boundary

Liberalism-paternalism boundary is the episode's problem of when law may restrict conduct that appears self-regarding. In [[100-fa-lv-de-bei-lun-xie-tian-xie-di-xing-hao-wo-men-hai-you-luo-xiang-733477302]], [[JohnStuartMill|John Stuart Mill / 穆勒]] supplies the liberal principle that society should not interfere with acts affecting only the actor, but the episode then tests that principle with drug use, helmets, seatbelts, self-injury, and voluntary slavery.

The source argues that the boundary cannot be solved by either total freedom or unlimited "for your own good" control. Soft paternalism may be justified where consent, information, or rational capacity is compromised, while hard paternalism risks turning adulthood into permanent tutelage.

## Key Claims
- Liberalism protects the right to choose imperfectly, not only the right to choose well.
- Self-harm examples become difficult because conduct that looks private may affect families, medical systems, or future autonomy.
- A ban on voluntary slavery shows that even liberal freedom can contain a non-waivable freedom baseline.
- Paternalist arguments need narrow justification because "for your own good" can expand without limit.
