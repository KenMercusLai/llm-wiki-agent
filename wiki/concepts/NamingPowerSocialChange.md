# Naming Power In Social Change / 社会变革中的命名权

Naming power in social change is the episode's closing frame for why new words and roles can change how people process reality. [[132-zhenglun-yu-shuofu-women-weishenme-chaojia-zenme-cai-suan-ying-807583318]] uses "designated driver" and "代驾" as examples where naming a role gives a behavior social dignity, recognizability, and coordination value.

The source links naming to broader norm change. A harmful old practice can persist while it is tied to honor, romance, dignity, or belonging; it becomes easier to abandon when the public language recodes it as crude, backward, or shameful.

[[the-laws-of-the-office-revisited]] adds an adjacent visibility mechanism through [[AliceEvans]]. The episode's [[TruthfulSocialProof]] branch says people often change when they see others changing first, but the signal has to be real; fake norm claims can damage trust instead of moving behavior.

## Key Claims
- Naming can force attention to a pattern that previously passed as background.
- A named role can create status and make coordination easier.
- Social change often follows environmental change first, with language helping culture catch up.
- Shame can be a powerful norm lever, but it should be used with care because social shame can feel existential.
- Truthful visibility can support norm change when it shows real behavior rather than invented consensus.
