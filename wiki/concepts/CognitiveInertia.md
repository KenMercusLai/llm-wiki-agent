# Cognitive Inertia / 认知惰性

Cognitive inertia is the episode's explanation for why people often do not update beliefs when new evidence appears. In [[132-zhenglun-yu-shuofu-women-weishenme-chaojia-zenme-cai-suan-ying-807583318]], thinking hard is costly, so a person tends to make the smallest possible adjustment unless the issue feels personally important, risky, or rewarding enough to justify deeper processing.

The concept helps explain why public-event rumors persist. A distant observer may have little incentive to process correction evidence, especially when the rumor supplies entertainment, outrage, group belonging, or a simple moral story.

## Key Claims
- Belief change has a cost, so people often prefer low-effort repair of the old model.
- Personal relevance, risk, and reward can make someone more willing to stop and rethink.
- Cognitive inertia works with [[TribalTruth]] when changing a belief also threatens belonging.
- It also works with [[CognitiveDissonanceAndAccommodation]] because assimilation is cheaper than accommodation.

## Connections
- [[MotivatedBias]] - desire and identity can direct low-effort interpretation.
- [[ElaborationLikelihoodModel]] - persuasion depends partly on whether motivation triggers deeper processing.
- [[InformationCocoon]] - homogeneous information environments lower pressure to update.
- [[RationalHumility]] - discipline that counteracts easy model preservation.
