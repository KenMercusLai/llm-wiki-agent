# Drone Decoy Economics

Drone decoy economics is the use of cheap, unarmed, or lower-value drone-like systems to force defenders into costly, fast, and imperfect engagement decisions. [[tech-20260319-0319-mp-tech-pod-128-tech-20260319-0319-mp-tech-pod-128]] adds the concept through [[StaceyPettijohn]]'s description of Russia using smaller decoys that can look like larger armed [[Shahed136]]-style drones.

The value of a decoy comes from uncertainty. If defenders cannot confidently tell whether an incoming object is armed, they may spend expensive interceptors, reveal defensive positions, or overload human and sensor attention. That makes decoys an extension of [[DroneDefenseEconomics]] rather than a separate trick.

## Key Claims
- Decoys can be strategically useful even when they carry no explosives.
- The defender's decision window matters because slow classification can allow real threats through.
- Cheap decoys can increase the attacker's leverage by consuming defensive attention and munitions.
- Decoys make [[CounterDroneLayeredDefense]] harder because each layer needs some ability to classify, prioritize, and conserve expensive effects.
