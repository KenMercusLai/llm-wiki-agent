# Responsibility Versus Guilt / 责任与罪行区分

Responsibility versus guilt / 责任与罪行区分 is the frame [[103-ta-shi-cichuan-yewu-de-guang-ta-shi-hanna-alunte-737939789]] uses to explain the most painful part of [[HannahArendt|Hannah Arendt]]'s [[EichmannInJerusalem|《艾希曼在耶路撒冷》]] controversy. The episode says Arendt's criticism of some [[JewishCouncils|Jewish Councils]] was widely heard as victim-blaming, but the hosts read her as asking a different question: how responsibility works under coercive, murderous institutions.

The distinction matters because guilt is a legal and moral assignment for crimes, while responsibility can include judgment, cooperation, refusal, record-keeping, and role performance under constraint. In the source, keeping the distinction visible lets the wiki preserve two claims at once: [[NaziGermany|Nazi Germany]] bears responsibility for [[TheHolocaust|the Holocaust]], and people inside constrained systems may still face hard questions about what they helped normalize or administer.

## Key Claims
- Asking about responsibility is not the same as assigning equal guilt.
- Victim status should not erase personhood, agency, or tragic constrained choice.
- Coercive systems make responsibility harder to judge, but not meaningless.
- Legalistic obedience can become morally dangerous when it turns people into instruments of a system.
- The frame connects [[LawMoralityBoundary]] to political catastrophe: legality and guilt categories are necessary but do not exhaust moral inquiry.
