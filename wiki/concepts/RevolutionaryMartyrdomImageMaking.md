# Revolutionary Martyrdom Image-Making / 革命殉道图像制造

Revolutionary martyrdom image-making / 革命殉道图像制造 is the source's frame for how [[JacquesLouisDavid|Jacques-Louis David]]'s [[DeathOfMaratPainting|The Death of Marat]] transforms [[JeanPaulMarat|Jean-Paul Marat]] into a saint-like revolutionary victim. The painting's political force comes from selection: the body is beautified, the note is legible, charity is foregrounded, and [[CharlotteCorday|Charlotte Corday]] is absent.

The concept does not reduce art to propaganda. It asks how a compelling artwork can organize political sympathy by deciding which details are visible and which are suppressed. In this episode, the missing assassin matters as much as the dying revolutionary because the image makes Marat the sole moral subject of the scene.

The later visual rehabilitation of Corday reverses part of the arrangement. By placing Corday upright, calm, and visible, later paintings participate in [[HistoricalMemoryContest]] over whether the event means martyrdom, murder, punishment, or political refusal.

## Key Claims
- Martyr images can turn political violence into sacred-looking sacrifice.
- Omission is an active political choice: absent actors cannot claim motive, dignity, or moral complexity.
- Beautiful art can preserve a simplified memory even when later history complicates the subject.
- Counter-images can reopen historical judgment by restoring the excluded actor to view.
