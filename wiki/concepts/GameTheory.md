# Game Theory

Game theory is the study of strategic decisions where each actor's best move depends on what others do. [[how-to-win-a-penalty-shootout-with-game-theory]] adds a compact sports example: a penalty taker and goalkeeper are not choosing in isolation, because each side is trying to anticipate the other's direction, timing, and habits.

The episode's contribution is practical rather than mathematical. It shows that [[GameTheory]] can matter in a familiar event where execution skill is real but not sufficient; the best technical shot can become a poor strategic choice if it is too predictable.

[[106-bie-zai-haipa-chongtu-kuai-lai-xuexi-tanpan-shu-742539645]] adds a negotiation version of strategic interdependence. In that source, one side's offer, concession, and silence matter because the other side is inferring [[BATNA]], [[ReservationValue]], [[ZoneOfPossibleAgreement]], and intent from every signal.

## Key Claims
- Strategic settings require thinking about the opponent's beliefs, not only one's own strongest action.
- High-stakes expert environments can produce behavior closer to theory than low-stakes lab games.
- Data changes the game because it can reveal opponent tendencies but can also make the data user's own behavior legible.
- Negotiation adds information asymmetry: each party is trying to learn the other's alternatives, bottom line, priorities, and biases while protecting its own.
