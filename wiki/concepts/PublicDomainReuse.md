# Public Domain Reuse

Public domain reuse is the creative and commercial opportunity created when copyright expires and works can be used without licensing from the old rights holder. In [[betty-boop-excel-olympics-penny-isms-our-2026-valentines]], [[JenniferJenkins]] explains that the 1930 version of [[BettyBoop]] entered the public domain on January 1, 2026, allowing [[PlanetMoney]] to build a new Valentine card around the character.

The concept is the counterpart to [[IPOwnership]]. Ownership lets a company control reuse while rights remain active; public-domain entry returns some old works to a shared pool where new jokes, products, adaptations, and criticism become easier. The episode's mention of [[TheWaltDisneyCompany|Disney]]-linked copyright-term lobbying shows that the timing of public-domain entry is itself a political economy problem, not a neutral clock.

## Key Claims
- Public-domain entry can convert dormant or old cultural material into immediate new creative options.
- The same character can carry legal, commercial, and cultural meaning; Betty Boop is useful because she is recognizable and because the source frames her as unusually independent for early animation.
- Copyright-term extensions can delay reuse for decades, making public-domain availability a policy outcome as well as a legal status.
- Public-domain reuse differs from [[CopyrightPlatformConflict]] because the new use is lawful by status rather than a platform forcing a later settlement.
