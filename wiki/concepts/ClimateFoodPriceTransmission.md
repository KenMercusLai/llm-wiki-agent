# Climate Food Price Transmission

Climate food price transmission is the mechanism by which a climate shock moves through ecological conditions, commodity inputs, production systems, and final consumer prices. [[shangye-xiaoyang-45-eerninuo-yueda-yu-yuegui-1000164352]] adds a concrete seafood case: [[ElNino]] changes ocean upwelling, [[PeruvianAnchoveta|anchoveta]] populations fall, [[FishmealSupplyChain|fishmeal]] supply tightens, aquaculture feed costs rise, and salmon, shrimp, sea bass, sushi, and restaurant seafood can become more expensive.

The concept is useful because the consumer-facing item may be far from the original climate signal. Diners see salmon or shrimp prices, but the source traces those prices back to plankton, juvenile catch shares, government bans, feed formulas, and commodity concentration in [[Peru]], [[Chile]], and [[Ecuador]].

## Key Claims
- Climate-price links can be indirect: the bottleneck may be an intermediate input rather than the final crop or animal.
- Price transmission depends on the size of downstream demand; aquaculture's growth makes fishmeal shocks more important than in earlier decades.
- Resource-management responses can be part of the price pathway because bans and quotas change current supply to protect future stock.
- A restaurant or retailer can have strong local procurement discipline and still face upstream feed-cost shocks.
