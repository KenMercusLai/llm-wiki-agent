# Retail Shelf Appeal

Retail shelf appeal is the ability of a packaged product to make a shopper notice, understand, and pick it up under severe attention limits. In [[board-games-3-whats-in-a-name]], [[JamieWolanski]] describes the board-game version as a few feet and a few seconds: shoppers see many boxes and often decide quickly whether one is worth inspecting.

The concept extends the wiki's [[RetailShelfPlacement]] and [[PackagingAsProductExperience]] branches from CPG into board games. For [[SellMeASasquatch]], the title, creature theme, vending-machine cover idea, and gift/game-night use case all have to explain value before the buyer learns the full rules.

## Key Claims
- Shelf appeal is a communication constraint, not only an aesthetic preference.
- Box design and title must reduce shopper uncertainty before detailed product evaluation begins.
- Gift buyers need a product promise they can understand on behalf of someone else.
- A strong mechanic still needs [[ProductNamingAsPositioning]] because most shoppers will not read the rulebook first.
- Shelf appeal can conflict with [[CrossMarketNamingFriction]] when the most eye-catching local reference is less legible elsewhere.
