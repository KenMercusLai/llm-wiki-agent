# E234｜未来实拍电影还存在吗？与导演陆川聊聊AI给影视人的恐惧与自由

## Summary
This [[SiliconValley101]] episode uses [[ByteDance]] video generation, Hollywood copyright objections, and creator anxiety to examine how AI changes film and dubbing work. [[LuChuan]] argues that [[VideoModels]] and [[AIVideoProductionWorkflow]] can compress visual-effects previsualization from months into days, but that AI remains a tool rather than a guarantee of cinema-quality work. [[HuangYing]] adds the voice-performance boundary: [[AIDubbing]] can reproduce patterned delivery, but it struggles with subconscious timing, imperfection, and cooperative emotional judgment, while [[AIVoiceCloningRights]] remain legally and technically difficult. [[KESA]] and [[StoryStorm]] supply the independent-creator angle, showing why faster, lower-cost AI production feels liberating after traditional IP development cycles.

## Key Claims
- [[KESA]] moved from traditional screenwriting into AI content because long-video and IP development were slow, uncertain, and dependent on platform, director, actor, schedule, and production coordination.
- [[Midjourney]] and adjacent tools let KESA make the AI short 《加班夜》 in a weekend, turning an idea that would have been difficult to shoot into a 48-hour experiment.
- [[LuChuan]] says traditional visual-effects workflows require storyboards, animatics, previsualization, modeling, animation, asset rigging, lighting, and many teams; AI can turn prompts, images, and video models into rapid keyframes and previs.
- The episode treats AI's clearest production value as early visualization and communication, not as automatic replacement of every industrial film function.
- Lu Chuan warns that models aligned to mass taste can generate homogeneous faces and weaken authorial distinctiveness, especially when short-drama and low-quality volume content become training influence.
- Lu Chuan argues that feature films and long-form drama need [[IndustrialGradeFilmModels]], not only social short-video tools, because commercial film delivery depends on stable style, quality, and workflow integration.
- Huang Ying says strong dubbing depends on context-specific pauses, stress, hesitation, and emotional sharing between actors; these forms of "human subconscious" judgment are still hard for AI to reproduce.
- Huang Ying treats voice rights as comparable to likeness rights: contracts should specify whether a voice may be learned, trained on, cloned, or used by AI.
- AI voice infringement is hard to litigate when the other party denies copying or blends several speakers' voices, because legal proof may require technical evidence that is not yet stable in civilian use.
- Lu Chuan frames AI as a "ruler" for [[LiveActionFilmUnderAI]]: if AI can easily produce a scene, investors and creators should ask why a real crew must spend human labor and money on it.
- [[NanjingNanjing]] becomes the episode's example of what AI cannot easily replace: the collaborative human discovery of a line and performance moment between director and actor.
- The episode concludes that live action will not simply disappear, but may become more concentrated around unrepeatable performance, real locations, natural light, and the social experience of people making films together.

## Key Quotes
> "AI 只是笔、摄影机和剪辑台" - Lu Chuan's tool metaphor for AI in film creation.

> "人类的下意识" - Huang Ying's shorthand for the timing and emotional judgment missing from current AI dubbing.

> "观众能接受什么，市场就会制造什么" - Huang Ying's warning about audience taste and supply formation.
