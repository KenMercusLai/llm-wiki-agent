## Summary
This [[TheSocialRadars]] episode has [[JessicaLivingston]] and [[CarolynLevy]] interview [[SurbhiSarna]] about the path from a teenage ovarian-cyst scare to founding [[NVisionMedical|nVision Medical]], a medical-device company focused on [[OvarianCancerDiagnostics]] and [[FallopianTubeAccessDiagnostics]]. The source turns a mission-driven founder story into a concrete medtech sequence: personal exposure to diagnostic uncertainty, physician discovery, catheter design, FDA clearance, clinical studies, fundraising rejection, and the eventual [[BostonScientific]] acquisition. Its strongest contribution is the positive regulated-healthcare counterpoint to the wiki's [[Theranos]] branch: patient need matters, but it has to be translated through careful device design, clinical data, regulatory strategy, and commercialization capacity.

## Key Claims
- [[SurbhiSarna]] traces her founder motivation to severe ovarian cysts at age 13, when diagnostic uncertainty, unreliable blood tests for premenopausal patients, and biopsy-spread risk made [[WomensHealthDiagnosticGap]] personally visible.
- The episode frames [[OvarianCancerDiagnostics]] as a site-of-origin access problem: Sarna cites research suggesting many ovarian cancers begin in the fallopian tube, then compares progress in Pap smears, colonoscopies, and mammograms to the need to reach earlier disease locations.
- [[NVisionMedical|nVision Medical]] began with pre-prototype physician conversations, including a pitch to Sarna's own gynecologist and introductions to physicians at Stanford and UC Davis.
- [[PhysicianLedMedicalDeviceDesign]] shaped requirements around safety, intuitive handling, screen-directed use, and ergonomics for different physician hand sizes.
- The device concept became a self-navigating catheter using a linear everting balloon that could unfold into the fallopian tube, collect cells, and retract into a sheath to reduce contamination risk.
- [[CindyDAmicus|Cindy D'Amicus]] helped Sarna think through FDA strategy, while Sarna's first technical hire was a former boss with senior R&D and exit experience.
- Sarna says it took roughly a year and a half to raise the first $250,000, in a market where investors did not yet treat women's health as a major category and one dismissed the area as "bikini medicine."
- [[AnulaJayasuria]] helped introduce Sarna to investors; the first financing path involved a $125,000 commitment if she found a match, another $125,000 match, and then [[TimDraper]] matching the $250,000 to give the company $500,000 for prototype work.
- With that first $500,000, the company completed enough bench and animal-tissue testing to support a working prototype; by April 2013 it had raised $4.5 million.
- [[MedicalDeviceClinicalValidation]] became the credibility path: the company completed two clinical studies, received FDA clearances in 2015 and 2016, and then raised $12 million.
- In the episode's account, a 50-patient study found cancerous cells in all five cases where cancer was present in the fallopian tube, making the device materially more credible to strategic buyers.
- Sarna declined earlier life-changing acquisition interest because she wanted to show the device could work in patients with cancer; later she accepted [[BostonScientific]] after weighing mission, investor outcomes, commercialization risk, and acquirer trust.
- After acquisition, Sarna stayed with the product and helped presell hospital accounts, but COVID disrupted the planned April 2020 launch.
- [[WithoutADoubt]] is presented as a memoir title about mission certainty rather than total founder confidence: Sarna says she doubted many decisions, but not that patients needed the product.
- Sarna's later [[YCombinator]] work is linked to helping underrated founders see a path to building when privilege, category fit, or self-belief are missing.
