## Summary
This [[TheSocialRadars]] episode has [[JessicaLivingston]] and [[CarolynLevy]] interview [[DavidKirtley]] about building [[Helion]] from a fusion-physics effort into a company trying to sell low-cost, safe, clean electricity. Kirtley connects [[CommercialFusionPower]], [[FusionEnergyRecovery]], [[DeepTechProductFocus]], [[HardTechRegulatoryPathfinding]], [[PrecisionManufacturingAsStrategy]], customer agreements with [[Microsoft]] and [[Nucor]], and [[FounderMode]] into one hard-tech operating story. Its strongest synthesis is that Helion's challenge is not only making fusion work, but making fusion manufacturable, regulated, financed, focused, and deployable at power-grid scale.

## Key Claims
- [[DavidKirtley]] explains fusion as light isotopes fusing under extreme heat and pressure, with the mass difference released as energy through E=mc^2.
- [[Helion]] uses deuterium and helium-3 and says its target product is generators for low-cost, clean, safe, baseload electricity.
- Kirtley frames fusion's hard part as reaching roughly 100 million degrees and about 1,000 atmospheres while avoiding the radioactive byproduct and meltdown profile associated with fission.
- [[SamAltman]] visited Helion, spent several days digging into the physics and business path, then encouraged the team to join [[YCombinator]].
- YC pushed Helion from broad work across plasma physics, materials, electricity, training, and space propulsion toward one product, making the episode a strong case for [[DeepTechProductFocus]].
- Kirtley says Helion has built seven fusion prototypes in roughly 10 years by using modern computing, fiber optics, power electronics, in-house manufacturing, and fast iteration.
- [[HelionPolaris|Polaris]], Helion's seventh-generation machine, is described as complete, running at full power, doing fusion for several months, and being optimized for higher output.
- [[FusionEnergyRecovery]] is central to Helion's technical bet: Kirtley says the company demonstrated 96% electricity recovery in 2014 and that Polaris is recovering more than 90% of input electricity.
- Kirtley treats the [[Microsoft]] power-purchase agreement and [[Nucor]] behind-the-meter plan as customer accountability, not merely public-relations proof.
- The source says [[HelionOrion|Orion]] is planned to deliver 50 MW to the grid for Microsoft through [[ChelanPUD]] and [[ConstellationEnergy]], while the Nucor plan targets a 500 MW industrial generator.
- [[HardTechRegulatoryPathfinding]] matters because Helion wanted fusion regulated differently from traditional nuclear fission and proactively worked with Washington regulators around its fifth-generation machine.
- [[PrecisionManufacturingAsStrategy]] matters because Helion's machines combine 40-foot physical scale, thousandths-of-an-inch alignment, nanosecond timing, high voltage, shielding, and fast manufacturing learning.
- Kirtley says Helion could sell helium-3 into medical-imaging and other markets, but avoids that side market because it does not solve the company's clean-electricity mission.
- The episode defines Helion's success as industrial deployment, not a single historic demonstration: Kirtley says the company would fail if it only proves fusion once or builds one power plant.
