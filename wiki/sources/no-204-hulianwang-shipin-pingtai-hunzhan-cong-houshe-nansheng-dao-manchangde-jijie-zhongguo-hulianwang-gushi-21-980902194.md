## Summary
This [[Banlatte]] China-internet-history episode treats Chinese online video as a long transition from local files, codecs, and [[P2PStreaming]] into platformized entertainment. It follows [[Youku]], [[Tudou]], [[PPLive]], [[PPS]], [[BaofengYingyin]], [[IQIYI]], [[TencentVideo]], [[MangoTV]], [[SohuVideo]], and later [[Douyin]]/[[Hongguo]] pressure, arguing that the industry's main constraint shifted from technology and upload ecology to copyright, exclusive content, membership conversion, production budgets, and large-company backing. The source also gives the wiki a creator-supply branch: [[HousheNansheng]], [[HuGe]], [[JiaoshouYiXiaoxing]], [[LuZhengyu]], and [[YangYuJiaozi]] show how early UGC platforms fed later film, animation, and variety-show industries.

## Key Claims
- Before web video platforms, Chinese users had already learned to watch video on computers through VCDs, [[BaofengYingyin]]-style all-format players, RMVB files, downloads, and local playback.
- [[P2PStreaming]] and Flash web playback created the first true platform era: video moved from files and tools into upload sites, browser playback, and live-streaming infrastructure.
- [[Youku]] won early through speed, stability, servers, bandwidth, and CDN investment, while [[Tudou]] represented a stronger UGC and creator-community route.
- The 2008 online-audiovisual regulatory turn and copyright lawsuits changed the game: platforms could no longer rely on pirated or user-uploaded professional content at low cost.
- [[OnlineVideoCopyrightRegime]] made content budgets decisive, leading to expensive drama/variety rights, exclusive licensing, and the 2012 [[Youku]]/[[Tudou]] merger.
- Early UGC culture was not a side story. [[UGCToProfessionalCreatorPipeline]] connects [[HousheNansheng]], [[HuGe]], [[JiaoshouYiXiaoxing]], [[LuZhengyu]], and [[YangYuJiaozi]] to later professional film, animation, and web variety production.
- [[IQIYI]] under [[GongYu]] treated content and exclusivity as the core problem from the start, then used [[MaDong]], studio production, "Qi Pa Shuo," and "The Lost Tomb" to turn web variety, web drama, and membership into growth engines.
- [[VideoMembershipModel]] became real for Chinese long video when exclusive web dramas and ahead-of-schedule viewing converted users from free streaming into paid subscription behavior.
- [[TencentVideo]] arrived late but caught up through [[Tencent]] funding, [[SunZhonghuai]]'s content push, Penguin Pictures, and large-scale copyright and self-produced drama spending.
- [[MangoTV]] shows a different route: broadcast-system content ownership, refusal to resell Hunan TV programming, and exclusive variety supply created a profitable long-video platform earlier than most peers.
- [[ChineseLongVideoPlatformEconomics]] stayed hard even after scale: content costs, user expectations, bit-rate complaints, weak network effects, and limited pricing power kept platforms under pressure.
- [[Douyin]], [[Hongguo]], [[AIShortDrama]], and [[ShortDramaEconomics]] do not eliminate long-form drama and variety, but they reset attention, cost, feedback loops, and the competitive frame for [[PlatformizedDramaProduction]].
