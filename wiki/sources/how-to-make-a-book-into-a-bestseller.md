## Summary
This [[PlanetMoney]] episode closes the show's book-launch arc after [[inside-a-book-auction]] and [[bookstore-economics]] by examining the [[NewYorkTimesBestsellerList|New York Times bestseller list]] as both market signal and demand engine. It follows the [[PlanetMoneyBook]] through launch week, pre-orders, live-event bundling, publicity, a poster-fulfillment problem, and the moment [[TomMayer]] at [[WWNorton|W. W. Norton]] tells the team the book reached number three on two Times lists.

The episode argues that bestseller status is not a transparent raw-sales scoreboard. Through [[LauraMcGrath]], [[GeneShepard]], [[JacquelineSusann]], [[WilliamPeterBlatty]], [[PatrickHealy]], and [[RachelSalzman]], it presents bestseller ranking as an opaque institution shaped by reporting-store secrecy, first-week timing, bookstore placement, bulk-buying detection, book laundering, and the professional upside attached to the label.

## Key Claims
- The [[NewYorkTimesBestsellerList|New York Times bestseller list]] has unusual power because appearance on it can create additional bookstore placement, online visibility, advertising copy, status, and future sales.
- [[BestsellerListOpacity]] protects the list from manipulation by hiding reporting stores and weighting, but that same secrecy encourages authors and publishers to infer and optimize around the system.
- [[GeneShepard]]'s [[ILibertine|I, Libertine]] prank shows that perceived demand can create real demand when readers, stores, and publishers begin responding to a nonexistent book.
- [[JacquelineSusann]]'s [[ValleyOfTheDolls|Valley of the Dolls]] campaign suggests that where first-week sales happen can matter, because suspected reporting stores become strategic targets.
- [[WilliamPeterBlatty]]'s lawsuit over [[LegionNovel|Legion]] clarified that the Times list is treated as editorial judgment rather than a guaranteed accounting of raw sales.
- Modern nonfiction bestseller gaming often uses [[BulkBookBuying]] or [[BookLaundering]], where organizations or hired firms buy books in patterns designed to look less like mass purchasing.
- [[BestsellerSpeakerEconomy]] gives nonfiction authors a reason to buy their way onto the list, because bestseller status can raise speaking, consulting, and thought-leadership pricing.
- [[PreOrderLaunchConcentration]] shaped the Planet Money launch: pre-orders counted toward first-week sales, while live-event tickets could include individual book purchases through likely reporting bookstores.
- A poster incentive intended to increase pre-orders briefly became a [[ProductLaunchUnderConstraint]] problem when missing-poster confusion drove early one-star reviews.
- The [[PlanetMoneyBook]] reached number three on both the print hardcover nonfiction list and the combined print and e-book list, but the episode frames that success as a starting point rather than guaranteed long-term profitability.

## Key Quotes
> "by hook or by crook" - the episode's frame for attempts to reach the list.

> "protected editorial content" - the legal frame from the Blatty lawsuit.

> "number three" - the launch-week result for the Planet Money book.

## Contradictions
- No direct contradiction found.
