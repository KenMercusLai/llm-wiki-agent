# 114.又到持螯赏菊时，聊聊螃蟹的趣闻史话

## Summary
This [[MihuanChishu|蜜獾吃书]] episode treats [[Crab|螃蟹]] as a seasonal food that has accumulated tools, place names, etymologies, literary scenes, strange-tale material, Buddhist stories, and folk jokes. It extends the wiki's [[FestivalFoodMaterialHistory]] branch by moving from Mid-Autumn crab references into a full cultural history of Chinese crab eating, from early records and "蟹灾" to dazha-crab naming, Song literati taste, [[HongLouMeng|《红楼梦》]] crab poems, and moral discomfort over killing. Its core synthesis is that one edible animal can become [[CrabCulturalSymbolism]]: delicacy, pest, monster, legal defendant, literary prop, ethical problem, and popular political metaphor.

## Key Claims
- [[Crab|Crab]] culture in China is not only culinary; names such as "郭索", place names, Shanghai's "沪" etymology, catching tools, and old records of crab fear make it a [[MaterialHistoryNarrative]] object.
- Early sources frame crabs unevenly: they can be food, tribute, sauce, giant sea monster, or agricultural pest competing with rice before later becoming a managed aquaculture resource.
- The episode keeps the origin of "大闸蟹" unresolved, presenting cooking-method and catching-tool explanations as plausible but not fully settled.
- [[JinPingMei|《金瓶梅》]] and [[HongLouMeng|《红楼梦》]] turn crab banquets into literary scenes where food, seasonal ritual, class atmosphere, poetry, and character judgment meet.
- [[LiQi|李琪]]'s [[XunXieshu|《讯蟹书》]] and crab寓言 show [[AnimalMoralProjection]]: humans use crab bodies and behavior to condemn, defend, mock, or praise human moral tendencies.
- [[HuangTingjian|黄庭坚]]'s love of crabs despite Buddhist compassion makes eating a case of [[EatingAndKillingMoralAmbivalence]] rather than a simple celebration of taste.
- Buddhist birth stories and their Chinese variants connect crabs to [[StoryMotifTransmission]]: the crab can be prey, trickster, rescuer, or punisher depending on the story version.

## Key Quotes
> "郭索" - crab alias used to introduce older literary and visual references.

> "一蟹不如一蟹" - proverb used to connect crab lore with decline narratives and courtly anecdote.

> "无肠公子" - folk nickname that becomes a moral pivot in the crab-defense material.
