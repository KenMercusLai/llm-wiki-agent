# 135.闲聊伟大作家们的八卦（第三弹）

## Summary
This [[MihuanChishu|蜜獾吃书]] episode continues the great-writer gossip series through [[MarkTwain|Mark Twain / 马克·吐温]], [[HGWells|H. G. Wells / H. G. 威尔斯]], [[JeanPaulSartre|Jean-Paul Sartre / 萨特]], and [[AynRand|Ayn Rand / 安·兰德]]. It uses funny, embarrassing, and politically uncomfortable anecdotes to extend [[LiteraryGossipAsContext]], [[AuthorMythDeflation]], and [[ClassicReadingComplexity]], while ending with a warning against treating any "ism" as a universal key to life.

## Key Claims
- The episode explicitly frames gossip as demythologizing rather than cancellation: literary achievement can coexist with vanity, cruelty, bad politics, sexual hypocrisy, bodily absurdity, and self-contradictory doctrine.
- [[MarkTwain|Mark Twain / 马克·吐温]] is presented as a taboo-breaking comic writer whose scatological humor, public speaking, inventions, financial failures, delayed autobiography, and friendship with [[NikolaTesla|Nikola Tesla / 尼古拉·特斯拉]] complicate a clean textbook image.
- [[HuckleberryFinn|Adventures of Huckleberry Finn / 《哈克贝利·费恩历险记》]] becomes a case where controversy, banning, racial language, and sales success sit together rather than resolving into one simple verdict.
- [[HGWells|H. G. Wells / H. G. 威尔斯]] is treated as both a major science-fiction founder and a bundle of ideological contradictions: socialist self-description, anti-Nazi reputation, alleged antisemitism, eugenic thinking, pacifist identity, war-game imagination, and sexual freedom all collide.
- Wells's works, including [[TimeMachine|The Time Machine / 《时间机器》]], [[WarOfTheWorlds|The War of the Worlds / 《世界大战》]], and [[InvisibleManHGWells|The Invisible Man / 《隐身人》]], extend [[ScienceFictionRealism]] by showing how speculative prediction, social theory, and personal politics can be entangled.
- The episode uses Wells's relationships with [[RebeccaWest|Rebecca West / 瑞贝卡·韦斯特]], [[MarthaGellhorn|Martha Gellhorn / 玛莎·盖尔霍恩]], [[AmberReeves|Amber Reeves / 安伯·里夫斯]], and [[MuraBudberg|Moura Budberg / 穆拉·巴德伯格]] to expose the gap between declared sexual liberation and power inside actual relationships.
- [[JeanPaulSartre|Jean-Paul Sartre / 萨特]] is presented as an important philosopher whose public hero image is weakened by the source's account of wartime nonresistance, Communist alignment, silence about Soviet abuses, drug use, and messy intimate life with [[SimoneDeBeauvoir|Simone de Beauvoir / 西蒙娜·德·波伏娃]].
- [[BeingAndNothingness|Being and Nothingness / 《存在与虚无》]] and [[NoExit|No Exit / 《禁闭》]] are named as major works that flourished during occupation-era Paris, complicating Sartre's postwar heroic memory.
- [[AynRand|Ayn Rand / 安·兰德]] is treated as both personally influential and intellectually dangerous: her [[Objectivism|Objectivism / 客观主义]] and rational self-interest attracted readers and later business elites, but the episode highlights the mismatch between her freedom doctrine and her reaction to [[NathanielBranden|Nathaniel Branden / 纳撒尼尔·布兰登]]'s later relationship.
- The closing warning becomes [[IdeologicalTotalizationRisk]]: a doctrine can help a young reader feel strength, but the same doctrine becomes toxic when followers extract only the most aggressive part and use abstract concepts to simplify real people.
