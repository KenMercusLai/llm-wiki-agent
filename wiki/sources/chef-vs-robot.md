## Summary
This [[PlanetMoney]] episode turns a Philadelphia Chinatown taste test into a concrete case of [[RestaurantAutomation]]. [[KennyPoon]]'s [[RobbyWokbot]] at [[Instafoods]] competes against [[PhuongHoangChiang]] from [[TingWong]] on beef chow fun, fried rice, and wok stir-fried beef, while [[SholaOlunloyo]] judges taste and identifies which dishes came from the human chef.

The episode's economic frame is [[RobotChefCostQualityTradeoff]]: a robot can lower skill requirements, training time, staffing stress, and per-hour cost, but human wok technique still wins when [[WokHei]], flavor nuance, and craft tradition matter. [[DaronAcemoglu]] supplies the labor-economics branch by separating [[AutomationDisplacementEffect]] from [[AutomationReinstatementEffect]].

## Key Claims
- [[RobbyWokbot]] is a partially automated wok machine: [[KennyPoon]] still adds ingredients, while the machine spins, heats, sauces, and washes itself.
- [[PhuongHoangChiang]] represents high-skill human cooking: the episode says he has 13 years of chef experience, earns $35 an hour, and can cook 20 to 30 dishes per hour.
- [[WokHei]] is the key culinary difference the human side defends: intense wok heat creates caramelized flavor that diners may notice in dishes such as beef chow fun.
- [[RestaurantAutomation]] becomes attractive because restaurants are labor-intensive, margins are thin, and staffing skilled kitchen roles can be difficult.
- The episode cites [[DaronAcemoglu]] for the distinction between [[AutomationDisplacementEffect]], where robots take over tasks workers once did, and [[AutomationReinstatementEffect]], where automation creates or reshapes complementary work.
- The source says one new robot per thousand workers in automation-heavy industries from 1990 to 2007 reduced employment by about three workers and lowered wages by about 0.4 percent.
- [[SholaOlunloyo]] correctly identifies the human-made and robot-made dishes in all three tests, suggesting the food differences are detectable in this setting.
- The human chef wins beef chow fun and wok stir-fried beef, while the robot wins fried rice.
- The final tradeoff is economic as much as culinary: the episode says Robby costs about $5 an hour to rent and can produce 15 servings in the time a human chef makes four.

## Key Quotes
> "a dish without wok hei is just a regular dish" - the human-chef side's core taste claim.

> "hiring, staffing, and training costs have gone down" - Kenny Poon's operational case for Robby.

> "consumers will decide these trade-offs" - the source's closing market-selection frame.
