## Summary
This [[PlanetMoney]] episode follows [[AbelBrodeur]] and his attempt to make social-science research more reproducible through the [[InstituteForReplication]] and the [[ReplicationGames]]. It turns the [[ReplicationCrisis]] into an incentive-design story: researchers face pressure to publish novel significant results, and that pressure can reward [[PHacking]], weak documentation, and fragile findings.

The episode's strongest contribution is its practical enforcement model. [[ReplicationPackage|Replication packages]], [[Preregistration]], and transparency rules help, but Brodeur argues that research behavior changes more when authors believe independent teams may actually inspect code, data, and robustness.

## Key Claims
- [[AbelBrodeur]]'s smoking-ban project exposed how easy it is to keep changing an analysis until it clears a [[StatisticalSignificanceThreshold]].
- The source treats [[PHacking]] as an incentive problem, not only as individual misconduct: journals prefer novel significant results, while null findings are harder to publish.
- Brodeur and colleagues found a cluster of published results just on the statistically significant side of the 5 percent threshold, consistent with publication filtering, analytical tweaking, or both.
- Journal requirements for [[ReplicationPackage|replication packages]] and [[Preregistration|preregistration]] improve transparency, but the episode argues that they are not enough if no one checks the underlying work.
- The [[InstituteForReplication]] gave Brodeur an institutional identity that made authors more likely to share data and code.
- [[ReplicationGames]] turn research checking into a hackathon-like event where teams reproduce code, inspect data, and then run [[RobustnessChecks]].
- The Montreal game had 71 replicators and 14 main teams; most papers had no major issue, while two teams found more serious problems.
- The episode distinguishes simple reproduction from robustness analysis: rerunning author code is more objective, while judging alternative specifications requires domain and methodological judgment.
- One government-trust paper had documentation and missing-variable problems, showing how supposed raw data and actual analysis files can diverge.
- A cartel paper's result became insignificant when one cartel was removed, but the original authors argued that the removed cartel was central to the hypothesis.
- Brodeur gives authors a chance to respond before serious findings become public, which makes replication less accusatory and helps protect junior replicators.
- The episode frames post-publication errors as a system failure because peer review and journal processes should catch documentation, code, and robustness problems before citation and dissemination.
- [[ResearchIntegrityIncentives]] are the central fix: the expected chance of being checked may matter more than harsh punishment after a problem is found.
