## Summary
This [[PlanetMoney]] episode uses [[Chevron]]'s unusual continuity in [[Venezuela]] to explain how an agrarian coffee exporter became an early [[Petrostate]] and later a severe [[PoliticalResourceCurse]] case. It follows the story from the 1922 [[LakeMaracaibo]] gusher and foreign oil concessions through [[DutchDisease]], the 50-50 revenue bargain, [[OPEC]], [[OilNationalization]], [[PDVSA]], Chavez-era control, U.S. sanctions, and Chevron's remaining foothold.

The episode's main claim is not that oil is inherently good or bad. Through [[TerryKarl]]'s framing, it argues that Venezuela's oil became damaging when political offices, corporate executives, and weak institutions converted a valuable resource into dependence, rent distribution, corruption, and repeated boom-bust exposure.

## Key Claims
- Venezuela's oil discovery at [[LakeMaracaibo]] displaced an older coffee-export economy and helped make the country an early full [[Petrostate]].
- Oil inflows strengthened the currency and made coffee less competitive, making [[DutchDisease]] a core mechanism in the country's loss of export diversity.
- [[JuanPabloPerezAlfonso]] helped negotiate the 50-50 agreement that forced foreign oil companies to share more revenue, foreshadowing later [[OilConcessionBargaining]], producer coordination, and national control.
- Venezuela helped found [[OPEC]] with Iran, Iraq, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait, extending domestic oil nationalism into international [[OilProducerSupplyCoordination]].
- The 1976 nationalization created [[PDVSA]] and turned companies such as [[Chevron]] and [[ExxonMobil]] into contractors under state control.
- Oil wealth built jobs, infrastructure, and middle-class opportunity, but [[MiguelTinkerSalas]]'s account of [[CreolePetroleum]]'s company camp shows that modern oil enclaves also preserved social hierarchy through [[OilCompanyEnclave]] structures.
- [[HugoChavez]] redirected oil profits toward poor Venezuelans while tightening state control over oil companies, eventually taking employees and equipment; [[ExxonMobil]] and [[ConocoPhillips]] left while Chevron stayed.
- Under [[NicolasMaduro]], U.S. sanctions, damaged equipment, lost expertise, and degraded fields made Venezuela harder to rebuild despite Chevron's license and continued production.
- Chevron argued to [[UnitedStates|U.S.]] officials that an American exit would leave room for [[China|Chinese]] companies, turning its commercial case into a geopolitical-footprint argument.
- The episode frames Venezuela as a [[PoliticalResourceCurse]]: oil's outcome depends on governance, institutional discipline, and executive choices, not on geology alone.
