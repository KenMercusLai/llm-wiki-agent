## Summary
This [[WhatsNextKejiZaozhidao]] episode records an AWS Summit Shanghai discussion with [[YuYi]] and [[CangShifu]] on whether AI agents make [[OnePersonCompany]] work practical. The source's balanced claim is that AI lowers the starting threshold for solo founders: one person can build, publish, test, and operate a minimum loop before deciding whether to recruit partners. It also argues that AI compresses team problems into the founder rather than removing them, so acquisition, trust, compliance, finance, attention management, and agent supervision become more concentrated.

## Key Claims
- [[AmazonWebServices]] uses the [[FromIdeaToFrontier]] accelerator to frame Chinese-language one-person founders as a target group for AI-era cloud, training, and startup support.
- The episode treats OPC as a startup phase rather than a permanent refusal to build a team: a solo operator can go from 0 to 10, then may need a small team when supply chains, users, compliance, and delivery complexity rise.
- [[YuYi]] argues that non-technical builders should "eat their own dog food" by turning their own workflows, pain points, and content systems into tools before generalizing them for others.
- [[CangShifu]] says non-technical people working with AI still have to learn requirement definition, testing, and solution search, because they become responsible for judging whether AI output is usable.
- Skills, plugins, and MCP-like components can become valuable, but the episode treats raw 2C skill monetization as immature; 2B customization, cooperation, and service packaging may be more realistic.
- AI does not change the basic need for customer acquisition and repeat purchase. The solo founder still needs public proof, self-media, community distribution, conversion hooks, and a revenue path.
- Trust becomes scarcer when AI makes content and product change fast; users may rely more on concrete people, personal brands, long-term recognition, and relationships.
- AI can help one person run more functions, but commercializing a product still brings company, KYC, tax, finance, contract, and offline trust work that cannot be blindly delegated.
- [[YuYi]] uses many parallel agents but keeps red lines around deletion, protocol changes, spending, and socially damaging actions; [[CangShifu]] runs fewer concurrent tasks and reviews them before errors compound.
- The episode contrasts two agent philosophies: Yu Yi treats agents as partners or future organizational members with decision rights, while Cang Shifu emphasizes reliable tools, workflow control, and delivery certainty.
- Agent infrastructure remains incomplete for organizations: inter-agent communication, trust, privacy, security, multimodal collaboration, and AI-native work platforms are still underdeveloped.
- Traditional venture financing may fit OPC poorly because investors often expect scale, staffing, legal clarity, and exit paths; sponsorship, revenue sharing, incubation, and later equity negotiation are presented as alternative experiments.
- The closing synthesis is that the realistic near-term form may be a team of fewer than five people, each carrying a group of agents, rather than one person permanently doing everything alone.
