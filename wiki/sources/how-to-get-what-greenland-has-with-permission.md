## Summary
This [[PlanetMoney]] episode uses [[DonaldTrump]]'s renewed claims about [[Greenland]] to separate three issues that are often collapsed together: rare-earth mineral supply, Arctic military geography, and the legitimacy of acquiring inhabited territory. It argues that the [[UnitedStates]] can pursue mineral security and defense access through processing capacity, trade, infrastructure investment, and agreements with [[Greenland]] and [[Denmark]], rather than buying or controlling the island. The episode's recurring theme is [[StrategicAccessWithoutAnnexation]]: strategic resources and locations can matter without making formal ownership the right instrument.

## Key Claims
- [[Greenland]] is strategically valuable because of rare earths, Arctic routes, and missile-defense geography, but the episode says that value does not imply that the [[UnitedStates]] needs to own or control it.
- The mineral problem is a [[RareEarthProcessingBottleneck]] as much as a mining problem: the source says roughly 90 percent of rare earths are processed in [[China]], and the U.S. lacks the capability to turn heavy rare earths into many needed defense and industrial inputs.
- [[GracelandBaskaran]] argues that Greenland is not a near-term mineral fix because Arctic mining would require roads, rail, ports, electricity, long timelines, and very large capital outlays.
- The episode says [[Brazil]], [[Australia]], [[SaudiArabia|Saudi Arabia]], and [[India]] are more plausible near-term supply or processing partners than Greenland.
- [[Greenland]] and [[Denmark]] have both said Greenland is not for sale, and the source emphasizes that Denmark cannot simply sell Greenland because Greenland is self-governing and inhabited.
- The episode links Greenland's land system to [[Inuit]] norms in which land is shared and permission to build does not become private ownership of the land underneath.
- [[DanielImmerwahr]] uses [[HowToHideAnEmpire]] and examples such as [[GuantanamoBay]] to argue that modern strategic control often happens through bases and agreements rather than formal annexation.
- The source says the U.S. already has extensive defense access in Greenland dating to 1951, making [[GreenlandStrategicAccess]] a live arrangement rather than a future conquest problem.
- [[SahaUllsvig]] rejects framing Greenlanders as passive spectators, stressing Greenland's trade history, political agency, and refusal to be treated as a resource package for a new colonizer.

## Key Quotes
> "land is not for sale" - the source's description of the Greenlandic land norm.

> "not in the market for a new colonizer" - [[SahaUllsvig]]'s final agency frame.

> "having geology is not the same as having mineral security" - the episode's supply-chain lesson.
