# BOARD GAMES 3: What's in a name?

## Summary
This [[PlanetMoney]] episode follows the team's attempt to turn an economics-inspired card game into a mass-market retail product with [[ExplodingKittens]]. The game uses [[MarketForLemons|The Market for Lemons]] and [[AsymmetricInformation]] as its underlying mechanic, but the episode's real focus is [[GameMechanicToRetailProduct]]: a working game still needs a name, theme, art direction, box promise, and shopper legibility.

The source tracks the choice of [[SellMeASasquatch|Sell Me a Sasquatch]] as the U.S. title and theme. That decision links [[ProductNamingAsPositioning]], [[RetailShelfAppeal]], [[CryptidThemeLegibility]], and [[CrossMarketNamingFriction]]: the name is memorable for U.S. millennial shoppers and paranormal-culture nostalgia, but it creates translation and recognition risk in European markets.

## Key Claims
- A good mechanic is not yet a sellable product. [[ExplodingKittens]] treats rule validation as only the first half of the work; theme, name, art, and box appeal make the game legible to retailers and shoppers.
- The game adapts [[MarketForLemons|The Market for Lemons]] into partial-information card trading, turning [[AsymmetricInformation]] into distrust, bluffing, and party-game interaction rather than a lecture on economics.
- The [[PlanetMoney]] team initially favors a used-car theme because it fits the source paper, but [[ExplodingKittens]] warns that car imagery can mislead shoppers into expecting a racing or movement game.
- [[StephaniePesta]] presents three broad theme directions: misfit mascots, cryptids/magical creatures, and cats as salespeople.
- [[ProductNamingAsPositioning]] matters because a title has to teach tone and category quickly. The source treats "Sell Me a Sasquatch" as easier to say, remember, and enjoy than more generic swap-meet or car puns.
- [[JamieWolanski]] frames [[RetailShelfAppeal]] around the "three feet and three seconds" problem: big-box shoppers see hundreds of games and may only briefly scan a box before deciding whether to pick it up.
- The episode identifies the core big-box board-game buyer as often a millennial woman buying for that night's game night or as a gift, making shelf signal and giftability central to the product decision.
- The creative process unsettles the data-driven hosts because [[ExplodingKittens]] relies heavily on taste, what sticks, and internal judgment rather than formal focus groups.
- [[CryptidThemeLegibility]] gives the chosen title emotional texture through Sasquatch, Bigfoot, Yeti, Loch Ness Monster, fairy-tale creatures, and 1980s/1990s paranormal nostalgia such as [[TheXFiles|The X-Files]].
- [[CrossMarketNamingFriction]] appears when international teams worry that European shoppers may not know what a Sasquatch is, while translation could damage the title's rhythm and joke.
- The source says the game needs to be ready by July 2026 to reach shelves for Christmas 2026, so splitting U.S. and European titles would add production, art, naming, and factory complexity.
