## Summary
This [[MarketplaceTech]] episode explains [[SurveillancePricing]]: online retailers may show different prices to different customers based on personal or behavioral data, not only broad demand. [[KristinSchwab]] frames the issue through comments from [[JosephTurow]] and [[GarrettJohnson]], then tests Walmart.com with signed-in and anonymous browsing. The episode's strongest contribution is not proof that [[Walmart]] personalized the toothpaste price, but a clear example of how algorithmic retail pricing can become opaque to ordinary shoppers.

## Key Claims
- Digital shopping makes customer-specific pricing more precise and less visible than older merchant personalization.
- [[JosephTurow]] says surveillance pricing is legal and economically familiar, while still problematic because many consumers do not know it is happening.
- No one in the episode claims to know how common surveillance pricing is.
- Retailers may also use "surveillance discounting," such as member coupons or discounts after a customer leaves an item in a cart.
- [[GarrettJohnson]] says price discrimination can help some consumers and hurt others.
- Johnson says consumers trying to compare prices may need to obscure their identity by using incognito mode, another device, or different network conditions.
- Schwab's Walmart.com test found the same Colgate toothpaste listed at $3.74 anonymously and $3.96 while signed in with the same shipping address.
- Walmart says prices can vary by market and can shift as price matches and other real-time changes occur.
- The source concludes that shoppers often cannot tell whether a price reflects personalization, a discount, market variation, or a real-time algorithmic change.

## Key Quotes
> "surveillance pricing" - the episode's term for customer-specific prices based on collected data.

> "some kind of algorithmic something" - Schwab's cautious description of the Walmart price difference.
