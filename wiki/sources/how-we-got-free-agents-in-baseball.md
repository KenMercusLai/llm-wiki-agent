## Summary
This [[PlanetMoney]] episode, adapted from [[BusinessHistoryPodcast|Business History]], uses [[CurtFlood|Curt Flood]]'s challenge to [[MajorLeagueBaseball|Major League Baseball]]'s [[BaseballReserveClause|reserve clause]] to explain how baseball players moved toward [[BaseballFreeAgency|free agency]]. Flood lost [[FloodVKuhn|Flood v. Kuhn]] at the [[SupremeCourt|Supreme Court]], but the episode argues that his lawsuit and public campaign weakened the legitimacy of owner control.

The core economics frame is that the reserve clause created [[LaborMonopsony|labor monopsony]]: teams could hold a player's rights, trade him, or leave him with no viable MLB employer. The episode connects that market power to antitrust law, race, collective bargaining, arbitration, player revenue share, and the continuing [[SportsCompetitiveBalance|competitive-balance]] debate over payrolls and salary caps.

## Key Claims
- In 1969, [[CurtFlood|Curt Flood]] was traded from the [[StLouisCardinals|St. Louis Cardinals]] to the [[PhiladelphiaPhillies|Philadelphia Phillies]] against his wishes.
- The [[BaseballReserveClause|reserve clause]] gave teams continuing control over player rights, leaving Flood to accept the Cardinals' terms, accept the trade, or leave baseball.
- The episode frames the reserve clause as a [[LaborMonopsony|monopsony]] because one effective buyer controlled Flood's ability to sell his baseball labor.
- Flood, the [[MajorLeagueBaseballPlayersAssociation|players union]], and [[ArthurGoldberg]] pursued both litigation and [[PublicOpinionLitigationStrategy|public opinion]] as leverage.
- [[BowieKuhn]] rejected Flood's request to be treated as available to other clubs for the 1970 season.
- [[JackieRobinson]] testified for the player side after active players were afraid to do so.
- The [[SupremeCourt|Supreme Court]] ruled against Flood in [[FloodVKuhn|Flood v. Kuhn]], leaving baseball's antitrust exception and the reserve clause intact.
- Flood's legal loss still helped shift public opinion toward players and strengthened the labor position that later bargaining and arbitration used.
- In 1975, players challenged the contract language saying a club could renew a contract for "the period of one year"; arbitration accepted the one-year interpretation.
- [[BaseballFreeAgency|Baseball free agency]] increased player leverage and, according to the episode, helped move player compensation from less than a quarter of team revenue to around half.
- The episode says similar free-agency pressures appeared in the [[NationalBasketballAssociation|NBA]] and [[NationalFootballLeague|NFL]], though NFL free agency remained more limited until the 1990s.
- The closing competitive-balance debate contrasts MLB payroll freedom with NFL and NBA salary caps, using the [[LosAngelesDodgers|Los Angeles Dodgers]] as the episode's high-payroll example.

## Key Quotes
> "a well-paid slave is still a slave" - Flood's Howard Cosell interview frame in the source.

> "the period of one year" - the contract phrase later used by players to challenge indefinite reserve-clause renewals.
