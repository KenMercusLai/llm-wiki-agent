## Summary
This [[TheSocialRadars]] episode has [[JessicaLivingston]] and [[CarolynLevy]] interview [[BlakeScholl]] about building [[BoomSupersonic]] from an outsider supersonic-airliner thesis into an aerospace hard-tech startup. The source connects founder motivation, [[AllBusinessClassSupersonicModel|all-business-class supersonic economics]], [[RecursiveExpertRecruiting]], [[YCombinator]] storytelling, [[HardTechCustomerIntentProof]], [[XB1SupersonicDemonstrator|XB-1]], the [[RollsRoyce|Rolls-Royce]] engine crisis, [[CrisisForcedVerticalIntegration]], [[BoomlessCruise]], and [[SupersonicRegulatorySpeedLimit|supersonic regulation]] into one company-building arc. Its strongest synthesis is that Boom repeatedly turned disbelief into the next kind of proof: spreadsheet logic, expert recruiting, customer intent, prototype flight, engine ownership, and regulatory momentum.

## Key Claims
- [[BlakeScholl]] says [[BarcodeHero]] taught him that founder pain is often constant across startups, so the next company had to be something he deeply wanted to exist.
- His early [[BoomSupersonic]] thesis was not to solve sonic boom first, but to make ocean-crossing premium travel work through the [[AllBusinessClassSupersonicModel]].
- Scholl's spreadsheet argued that speed could substitute for lie-flat cabin space: if a flight is much shorter, a premium cabin can fit more seats while still serving high-fare travelers.
- Scholl was an aerospace outsider, but he used textbooks, Khan Academy, market models, a Stanford professor's feedback, and [[RecursiveExpertRecruiting]] to get enough technical and market credibility to start.
- Boom initially did not require overland supersonic flight, so [[BoomlessCruise]] appeared later after engine and flight-profile work changed what seemed possible.
- Scholl frames the 1973 U.S. supersonic rule as a [[SupersonicRegulatorySpeedLimit]] that blocked a plausible minimum viable product instead of regulating actual ground noise.
- [[YCombinator]] mattered because capital, narrative, and credibility were the largest early risks; the batch forced Boom to show a stronger de-risking story before Demo Day.
- [[RichardBranson]] and [[VirginGroup|Virgin]] supplied a critical [[HardTechCustomerIntentProof|customer-intent signal]] when Boom needed more than renderings and a weak airline LOI.
- [[XB1SupersonicDemonstrator|XB-1]] functioned as a technical prototype and team-training platform for the later [[OvertureSupersonicAirliner|Overture]] airliner.
- The [[RollsRoyce|Rolls-Royce]] break created a near-existential engine crisis, but Scholl says the in-house engine path later enabled lower development cost, [[BoomlessCruise]], and product options the supplier path would not have allowed.
- [[BrianChesky]]'s crisis advice helped Scholl interpret the Rolls-Royce break as a reason to become more deeply Boom rather than more conventional.
- Scholl says first passengers could be roughly four and a half years away if all goes according to plan, but this remains a source-perspective timeline rather than an independently verified certification forecast.
