## Summary
This [[MarketplaceTech]] episode asks whether AI in classrooms should be understood only as cheating or as a capability students must learn to judge. It centers on [[ChristyGerdhary]] of [[BabsonCollege]], whose faculty group [[TheGenerator]] explores values-based and care-centered responses to AI rather than only bans and detectors. The strongest contribution is [[AIWritingPedagogy]]: assignments that make AI collaboration visible, ask students to evaluate bot output, and push them toward work with personal, embodied, or multimodal meaning.

## Key Claims
- AI chatbots can generate essays, summarize texts, translate languages, and sometimes solve multi-step math problems, forcing educators to rethink assessment and evidence of learning.
- Some teachers ban AI, but Gerdhary argues students need to become "better than a robot" by understanding tool limits and output quality.
- [[TheGenerator]] began at [[BabsonCollege]] in fall 2023 and brought educators from more than 30 Boston-area institutions into shared AI teaching conversations.
- Values-based and care-centered approaches matter because Gerdhary says AI detectors tend to flag already marginalized students more often, creating [[AIDetectorBias]] risk.
- Prompt engineering is treated as writing: students need to know what makes a prompt, response, revision, or transformation effective.
- Remediation assignments can ask students to let AI transform their work, collaborate with AI while color-coding authorship, or create something AI cannot produce.
- The Bulgarian grandmother and clay rose example shows why physical making, personal memory, language, and family context can exceed generic text generation.
- The episode suggests that AI may push writing instruction beyond default five-paragraph essays toward public, multimodal, and judgment-rich communication.

## Key Quotes
> "be better than a robot" - Gerdhary's classroom rule for student AI literacy.

> "five-paragraph essays" - school writing form she says is uncommon outside school.
