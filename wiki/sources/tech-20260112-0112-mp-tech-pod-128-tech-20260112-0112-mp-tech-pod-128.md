## Summary
This [[MarketplaceTech]] episode has [[DavidBrancaccio]] describe rebuilding after L.A. wildfires destroyed more than 10,000 homes in Pacific Palisades and Altadena, including his own. The episode treats [[FireResilientConstruction]] as a set of practical tradeoffs among [[CrossLaminatedTimber]], mineral wool, stucco, concrete-filled wall systems, [[ThreeDPrintedHousing]], cost, speed, carbon, livability, and neighborhood memory.

The main wiki contribution is to move wildfire resilience from detection, suppression, utilities, and insurance into the house itself. It connects [[ClimateAdaptation]] and [[FireTechClimateResilience]] to material choices, building envelopes, off-site fabrication, and the question of whether faster rebuilding can preserve both safety and neighborhood character.

## Key Claims
- The episode frames post-wildfire rebuilding as an opportunity to improve fire resilience rather than simply recreate the same construction.
- [[DavidBrancaccio]] is rebuilding with [[CrossLaminatedTimber]], a pressed-layer wood material that the episode presents as fire-resistant, structurally strong, off-site fabricated, and fast to assemble.
- Brancaccio's wall strategy combines CLT with mineral wool insulation and stucco, making [[FireResistantBuildingEnvelope]] a layered protection problem rather than a single-material fix.
- [[KLH]] and [[MercerMassTimber]] are used as examples that mass timber is already being used in larger institutional projects, not only individual homes.
- [[NateFoster]] says off-site manufacturing can let panels be crane-placed quickly and may produce 15% to 20% schedule savings in some projects.
- Faster framing matters to [[HousingAffordabilitySupplyMechanics]] because post-disaster neighborhoods need many homes rebuilt, not only one exemplary house.
- [[HeidiLewis]] is rebuilding with [[ConcreteFilledWallSystems]], a concrete-centered approach that the source presents as highly fire resistant but different from older neighborhood house forms.
- The episode treats concrete as a resilience material with a climate cost, because cement production is carbon intensive.
- Wood-based construction is framed as a possible carbon-storage route if the building lasts for a century or more.
- [[ThreeDPrintedHousing]] is discussed as a promising but less visible option in this neighborhood, with economics that may work better when multiple nearby owners share equipment.
- [[BiophilicDesign]] and references to older storybook cottages show that resilient rebuilding also has to account for light, nature, taste, memory, and neighborhood identity.

## Key Quotes
