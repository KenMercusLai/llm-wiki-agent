## Summary
This [[ShangyeJiushiZheyang]] episode uses snack-sized "deep sea fish" to explain why [[PeruvianAnchoveta|anchoveta]], [[FishmealSupplyChain|fishmeal]], [[ElNino]], and global aquaculture are tightly connected. It argues that [[Peru]]'s anchoveta fishery is a concentrated upstream input for farmed salmon, shrimp, sea bass, and restaurant seafood supply, so a climate-driven catch shock can move from plankton ecology into feed costs and consumer prices. The source extends [[ElNinoClimateRisk]] from general food-security risk into [[ClimateFoodPriceTransmission]] through a specific hidden commodity.

## Key Claims
- The episode says anchoveta are small, strong-smelling fish with limited direct table value, but they become valuable when processed into high-protein fishmeal and fish oil.
- [[Peru]] is described as a key fishmeal supplier: its anchoveta-derived fishmeal is said to account for about 20% of global supply, while [[Peru]], [[Chile]], and [[Ecuador]] together supply almost one-third.
- [[ElNino]] suppresses cold-water upwelling off the Peruvian coast, reducing nutrients and plankton, which can rapidly shrink the anchoveta population because the species has a short life cycle.
- The episode says global aquaculture now supplies more than half of fish consumed by humans, compared with less than one-quarter during the 1997-1998 strong El Nino period.
- Feed substitution has reduced fishmeal and fish-oil inclusion rates, but high-value species such as salmon and shrimp still need specific amino acids and Omega-3 inputs that cannot be eliminated without lowering feed quality.
- The source claims 2026 Peruvian restrictions moved from a 15-day ban in mid-May to an extension through June 10 and then an indefinite ban after catches were low and juvenile-heavy.
- It treats the 1972 Peruvian anchoveta collapse as a warning: the episode says overfishing plus strong El Nino cut catches from 12.4 million tons in 1970 to 4.4 million tons and helped turn a commodity shock into an industry and political crisis.
- The episode's consumer-price claim is that higher fishmeal costs can reduce output or raise prices for salmon, shrimp, sea bass, oysters, sushi chains, and other seafood buyers.
- [[FisheriesResourceManagement]] is presented as a short-term pain and long-term supply problem: banning catches worsens immediate scarcity but can prevent the loss of future breeding stock.
