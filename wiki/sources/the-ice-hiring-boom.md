## Summary
This [[PlanetMoney]] episode examines [[USImmigrationAndCustomsEnforcement|ICE]] as a rapidly scaling workplace and detention system. It connects [[ICEWorkforceExpansion]] to disputed training changes, field-training culture, supervisor signals, lawsuits, and the local economics of expanded immigrant detention.

The episode's strongest contribution is the link between federal enforcement policy and small-town development incentives. [[FolkstonGeorgia|Folkston, Georgia]] and [[CharltonCounty]] receive jobs and public revenue from a [[GEOGroup|GEO Group]]-run detention facility, but the source frames that money as morally fraught and politically fragile because it depends on shifting immigration priorities.

## Key Claims
- [[USDepartmentOfHomelandSecurity|DHS]] says 12,000 agents and officers joined [[USImmigrationAndCustomsEnforcement|ICE]] in the prior year, more than doubling the agency's ranks.
- The source says ICE recruitment included waived age requirements and signing bonuses of up to $50,000, making hiring speed part of the enforcement story.
- DHS says agents are targeting the "worst of the worst," while the episode says many people caught in the crackdown had no criminal convictions, some had legal status, and some U.S. citizens were taken into custody.
- The episode reports conflicting accounts of training: [[TriciaMcLaughlin]] said officers receive the same number of training hours, while NPR's DHS numbers indicated 14 weeks of training and documents released by [[RichardBlumenthal]]'s office appeared to show 250 fewer hours for new recruits.
- [[MarkBrown]]'s training-center account emphasizes crowd-control restraint, while [[SethStoughton]] and [[MatthewRoss]] argue that field training, peer expectations, supervisors, and agency accountability may shape conduct more than classroom hours alone.
- [[MatthewRoss]]'s research using [[DallasPoliceDepartment]] data found that recruits paired with more aggressive field training officers were more likely to use force for at least three years.
- The source says about 71,000 people were in detention, a record high, and that the administration wanted capacity to detain about 93,000 people at once while planning more than $38 billion for detention expansion.
- [[FolkstonGeorgia]] is used as the local-economics case: a large ICE facility expanded from 1,100 beds to capacity for up to 3,000 people, adding about 200 jobs while directing about $1 million to the county and city under a much larger federal contract with [[GEOGroup]].
- [[SavannahPollock]] and [[GlennHall]] both warn in different ways that relying on detention as economic development is unstable because the facility's future depends on immigration policy that can change with presidential politics.
