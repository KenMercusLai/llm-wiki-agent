## Summary
This [[PlanetMoney]] episode treats [[SportsTanking|tanking]] in the [[NationalBasketballAssociation|NBA]] as an incentive-design problem created by the league's draft rules. [[AdamSilver]] and [[ZachLowe]] frame the problem less as individual bad faith than as predictable behavior when losing can improve access to scarce star talent.

The episode compares the NBA's reverse-order draft and lottery tweaks with three larger alternatives: the [[DraftWheel]], [[GoldPlan]], and [[NoDraftPlayerMarket|abolishing the draft]]. Its central synthesis is [[SportsDraftIncentiveDesign]]: every fix shifts tradeoffs among [[SportsCompetitiveBalance]], fan experience, weak-team recovery, owner incentives, and [[SportsPlayerAutonomy]].

## Key Claims
- The source says NBA teams with the worst records receive the best chance at top draft picks, making intentional losing rational late in the season.
- [[AdamSilver]] describes tanking as an incentives problem and says the league is considering remedies.
- [[ZachLowe]] explains that the draft reward is especially powerful in basketball because one elite player can change a team's future more than in many other sports.
- The historical [[HoustonRockets]] example shows the reward side of tanking: in 1984, the team lost heavily late, selected Hakeem Olajuwon, and later won championships with him.
- The NBA introduced and repeatedly adjusted the draft lottery to reduce the certainty of reward for finishing last.
- The episode says the 2019 lottery change flattened odds and reduced the worst team's chance at the first pick from 25% to 14%.
- [[DraftLotteryTradeoff]] remains unresolved: lowering the reward for losing can also weaken the mechanism that helps genuinely bad teams improve.
- The [[DraftWheel]] removes the record-to-pick connection by assigning every team a predetermined path through draft positions, but it can leave bad teams waiting years for meaningful help.
- [[ProfessionalWomensHockeyLeague|PWHL]] adopted the [[GoldPlan]], where eliminated teams improve draft position by winning after elimination.
- The Gold Plan tries to make late-season games meaningful, but teams could still game it by losing early enough to be eliminated sooner.
- The [[NationalWomensSoccerLeague|NWSL]] abolished its draft in 2024, letting incoming players negotiate directly with teams under a salary cap.
- [[SamUys]] frames the no-draft model as a player-autonomy issue as well as a tanking fix, especially for young players and players concerned about where they are required to live.
- The episode ends without a perfect solution: each system trades off fan trust, weak-team hope, competitive balance, team market power, and player freedom.
