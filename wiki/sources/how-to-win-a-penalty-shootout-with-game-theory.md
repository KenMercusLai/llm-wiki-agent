## Summary
This [[PlanetMoney]] episode uses [[LionelMessi]]'s penalty record and the 2008 Champions League final between [[ChelseaFC]] and [[ManchesterUnited]] to explain [[GameTheory]] through football. Its core idea is [[MixedStrategy]]: penalty takers and goalkeepers must sometimes choose less comfortable options so the opponent cannot exploit a pattern. The episode turns [[IgnacioPalacioHuerta]]'s penalty database, [[StefanSzymanski]]'s explanation, and [[SimonKuper]]'s reporting into a practical lesson about [[StrategicUnpredictability]], [[DataDrivenPenaltyPreparation]], and pressure in [[FIFAWorldCup]] knockout matches.

## Key Claims
- Penalty kicks are simultaneous strategic contests because the taker and goalkeeper often commit before either can fully observe the other's choice.
- [[StefanSzymanski]] says a taker who always uses the strongest side becomes exploitable, so optimal play can require occasionally using the weaker side.
- The episode presents professional penalty kicks as a real-world setting where [[MixedStrategy]] predictions fit behavior better than many lab experiments.
- [[IgnacioPalacioHuerta]] built a large penalty database beginning in the 1990s, giving him pattern knowledge that many clubs did not yet have.
- Before the 2008 Champions League final, [[ChelseaFC]] asked Palacio Huerta for a report on [[ManchesterUnited]] penalties.
- The report's advice helped [[PetrCech]] save [[CristianoRonaldo]]'s penalty after Ronaldo paused in his run-up.
- Chelsea initially exploited [[EdwinVanDerSar]]'s tendency by repeatedly shooting to his left, but that pattern made Chelsea predictable too.
- After [[JohnTerry]] slipped and missed, [[EdwinVanDerSar]] signaled that he had detected Chelsea's pattern before [[NicolasAnelka]]'s kick; Anelka shot against the report's advice and van der Sar saved it.
- The episode argues that the report did not simply backfire: both sides entered a game-theory contest where data changed the strategic field.
- Since 2008, penalty reports, goalkeeper crib sheets, psychological preparation, and simulated shootout training have become more common.
- The source frames modern [[PenaltyShootoutStrategy]] as a blend of statistics, behavioral judgment, and [[PenaltyShootoutPressure]] management.
