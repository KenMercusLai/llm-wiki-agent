## Summary
This [[PlanetMoney]] episode follows [[ChristianStClair]] of [[FantagraphicsBooks]] after he believes two books printed in [[India]] are stuck on the bright pink container ship [[OneMajesty]] during a U.S.-[[Iran]] crisis around the [[StraitOfHormuz]]. The episode uses [[HamidHosseini]]'s account of an alleged [[SepahNavy]] permission process and per-barrel crypto-paid toll to explain [[MaritimeChokepointTolling]] and [[SanctionsEvasionCryptoPayments]]. [[RyanPetersen]] of [[Flexport]] then reframes the problem from one fee into [[ChokepointShippingConfidence]], [[GeneralAverage]], and the wider [[FreedomOfNavigationTradeOrder]] that lets long-distance trade feel routine.

## Key Claims
- At the time of the episode's reported recording, the source says the United States and Iran were still blocking traffic from leaving the Persian Gulf, while Iran claimed control over permission to transit the Strait of Hormuz.
- Christian St. Clair first believed Fantagraphics books were on the One Majesty after the ship was attacked, but later learned the books were still in India and then loaded onto another bright pink ship.
- Hamid Hosseini says an Indian oil-ship owner requested approval from the Sepah Navy by providing vessel, flag, destination, cargo, and crew information.
- Hosseini says the ship paid a toll of one dollar per barrel of oil in crypto before sailing toward India; the episode treats this as a sourced account rather than independently verified policy.
- Ryan Petersen says a toll could become another cost in trade, but the deeper shock is any weakening of the U.S.-backed freedom-of-navigation regime.
- Petersen also explains [[GeneralAverage]]: cargo owners can share costs when damage or sacrifice protects a voyage, so an incident can create financial exposure even when a specific container is safe.
- The episode lists possible future settlement structures: no toll, Iran collecting tolls, the United States collecting tolls, or both sides splitting toll revenue.

## Key Quotes
