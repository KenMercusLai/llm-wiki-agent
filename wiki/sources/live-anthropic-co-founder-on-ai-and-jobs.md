## Summary
This [[PlanetMoney]] live book-tour episode pairs a San Francisco interview with [[JackClark]] of [[Anthropic]] and a Seattle interview with [[DarylFairweather]] of [[Redfin]]. Clark frames frontier AI as a production, distribution, cybersecurity, and education problem: if [[Claude]]-like systems can do long knowledge tasks, society may need [[AIAutomationRedistribution]] and [[AICyberDefenseUtility]] rather than ordinary software pricing alone.

Fairweather uses [[EverydayBehavioralEconomics]], [[DynamicPricingFairness]], and [[FutureResidentHousingVoice]] to explain why companies hire economists, why surge pricing can clear shortages while still feeling unfair, and why housing affordability depends on adding supply and representing people excluded from current local zoning politics.

## Key Claims
- [[JackClark]] says AI progress may force society to reconceptualize capitalism if machines can design, test, manufacture, and sell products with little human involvement.
- Clark predicts that by April 2027, AI systems may be able to perform tasks that would take a person roughly 150 hours, including involved research, circuit design, cross-source synthesis, and software building.
- Clark says [[Anthropic]] has discussed the possibility that all of its code could eventually be written by [[Claude]]-like systems, shifting human work toward critique, review, and verification.
- The episode says Anthropic has shared a cyber-capable Claude system with roughly 40 companies and frames that capability as both a security opportunity and a risk.
- Clark argues some cyber-defense AI capability may need to work like a utility, potentially offered broadly, at cost, and without margin-maximizing incentives.
- [[DarylFairweather]] says economists inside companies can shape employee engagement, prices, and consumer-facing markets, not only academic or government policy.
- Fairweather treats dynamic pricing as a tradeoff: surge pricing can prevent shortages and invite supply, but wait lists or envy-free allocation may feel fairer to consumers.
- Fairweather argues that eliminating single-family zoning is a major housing-policy shift, but affordability changes slowly because new supply takes time.
- Fairweather's housing metaphor is musical chairs: without more homes, first-time buyers and future residents remain excluded even if current homeowners are already seated.

## Key Quotes
> "messages in a bottle" - Clark's description of his AI fiction as moral reflection from inside an AI lab.

> "true utility" - Clark's framing for socially important cyber-defense AI capability.

> "future generations will need housing" - Fairweather's reason for shifting attention beyond current homeowners.
