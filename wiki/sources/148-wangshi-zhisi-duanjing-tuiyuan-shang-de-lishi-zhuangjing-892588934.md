# 148. 王氏之死：断井颓垣上的历史妆镜

## Summary
This [[MihuanChishu|蜜獾吃书]] episode reads [[JonathanSpence|史景迁]]'s [[TheDeathOfWomanWang|《王氏之死》]] as a microhistory of [[Tancheng|郯城]], a poor Qing county where disaster, tax pressure, war, banditry, household hierarchy, and gender law converged around one unnamed woman's murder. It explains how Spence combines [[TanchengCountyGazetteer|《郯城县志》]], [[HuangLiuhong|黄六鸿]]'s [[FuhuiQuanshu|《福惠全书》]], and [[PuSongling|蒲松龄]]'s [[LiaozhaiZhiyi|《聊斋志异》]] to recover a world that official records barely let [[WangShiTancheng|王氏]] enter. The strongest synthesis is that the book's ghost stories and legal case point to the same problem: real women had almost no recognized will, while women who became ghosts, foxes, or other "nonhuman" figures could finally appear as agents.

## Key Claims
- [[TheDeathOfWomanWang|《王氏之死》]] is built from three uneven source types: a county gazetteer for events and statistics, a magistrate's manual for administrative and judicial practice, and [[LiaozhaiZhiyi|《聊斋志异》]] for social imagination, fear, and fantasy.
- [[Tancheng|郯城]] is not a neutral setting. The episode foregrounds the 1668 earthquake, famine, Manchu conquest violence, banditry, tax arrears, corvee obligations, and transport burdens as the material pressure behind the case.
- [[MicrohistoryFromHostileArchives]] matters because [[WangShiTancheng|王氏]] leaves almost no direct voice; the source has to read around a murder file, official interests, family reputation, and literary parallels.
- [[FictionAsHistoricalEvidence]] is controversial but productive here: [[LiaozhaiZhiyi|《聊斋志异》]] is not treated as literal fact, but as evidence for anxieties around hunger, wife sale, bandit households, female competence, sexual danger, and ghostly revenge.
- The episode reads stories such as 《促织》, 小二, 细柳, and 云翠仙 as fantasy mirrors of ordinary suffering: fictional women can manage businesses, preserve households, expose bad husbands, or take revenge, while real women remain bound by poverty and kinship.
- [[QingGenderLegalOrder]] tied sex, property, family honor, and punishment together. Adultery rules, widow chastity, inheritance limits, and patriarchal household authority made female desire legally dangerous.
- [[WangShiTancheng|王氏]]'s attempted flight with another man did not create a viable escape route. Once abandoned and returned, she faced law, poverty, shame, and a husband's damaged public standing.
- [[HuangLiuhong|黄六鸿]] solves the murder by combining material clues with local belief: the new mat, body marks, night witnesses, and a staged confinement in the [[Chenghuang|城隍]] temple all matter.
- The punishment leaves the episode's central moral imbalance intact. Ren kills his wife, tries to frame Gao, and receives only heavy beating because Wang is treated as an "adulterous wife" inside lineage logic.
- The ending gives [[WangShiTancheng|王氏]] more force as a corpse or possible ghost than she had as a living wife, extending the wiki's [[FemaleSelfPossession]] branch through a historical negative case.
