# 《恐龙庇护所》 / Dino Sanctuary

《恐龙庇护所》 / Dino Sanctuary is the manga recommended in [[146-bu-ai-zhuluoji-shijie-kuailai-konglong-bihusuo-dang-siyangyuan-886472126]], where [[MihuanChishu|蜜獾吃书]] presents it as a better dinosaur story than simple monster spectacle. In the source's summary, the manga is set in a parallel world where living dinosaurs exist, dinosaur enthusiasm has faded after a fatal accident, and the struggling Enoshima Dinosaur Park employs the new keeper Suma Suzume.

The episode emphasizes that the manga is not just dinosaur facts in comic form. It is a workplace-growth story about hard care labor, an animal-welfare story about individual needs, and a science-fiction story whose reconstructions remain answerable to [[EvidenceBoundPaleontologicalReconstruction]].

The named dinosaur cases define the work's appeal. The giganotosaur Komayuki hesitates before unfamiliar food; the troodontid Nicole suffers heat stress and a choking emergency; the triceratops Asho carries pressure, injury, and replacement by a more marketable animal; the hatchling Benkei forces Suzume to separate her own family wound from the animal's good; and later tyrannosaur, ankylosaur, pterosaur, and mosasaur sections broaden the park into a care system.

## Key Claims
- The manga's dinosaurs are treated as animals first: they can be afraid, attached, bored, stressed, sick, old, clever, or socially vulnerable.
- The workplace frame matters because enthusiasm is tested against cleaning, feeding, observation, danger, budgets, and organizational pressure.
- The park setting makes [[CaptiveAnimalCareLabor]] visible: even a lovable animal story has air conditioning, enclosure design, visitor traffic, merchandise, food stalls, veterinary care, and headquarters decisions.
- The manga's speculative biology works best when it keeps uncertainty visible rather than pretending that extinct animals can be reconstructed with total certainty.
- The source treats the work as suitable for dinosaur fans, younger readers, and early-career readers because it turns "liking animals" into a responsibility question.
