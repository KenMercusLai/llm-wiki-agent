# HOPE VI Program

HOPE VI was the federal public-housing revitalization program at the center of [[can-transforming-neighborhoods-help-kids-escape-poverty]]. The episode says the program operated from 1993 to 2010, demolished hundreds of distressed public-housing projects, and aimed to replace concentrated-poverty projects with more open, often mixed-income developments.

The wiki should treat HOPE VI as both evidence and warning. [[RajChetty]] and colleagues' findings make it a case for [[NeighborhoodOpportunityRevitalization]], but the same source stresses [[PublicHousingDisplacement]]: almost 100,000 public-housing units were demolished while only about 55,000 were rebuilt.

## Key Claims
- HOPE VI tested whether changing a neighborhood environment could improve long-term outcomes for low-income children.
- The program's gains were concentrated among children rather than adults.
- The strongest outcomes came where revitalized developments connected to more affluent surrounding neighborhoods.
- HOPE VI's displacement and unit-loss record prevents a simple pro-demolition policy lesson.
