# 《千与千寻》 / Spirited Away

[[SpiritedAway|《千与千寻》 / Spirited Away]] is named in [[180-ailisi-mengyou-xianjing-shijie-duo-huangdan-wo-yeshi-ziji-de-zhuzai-xia-975539611]] as a modern otherworld story that can be compared with [[AliceInWonderland|《爱丽丝梦游仙境》]]. The episode uses it to show how a child entering a strange rule-world remains a durable narrative pattern.

For the wiki, the film extends [[OtherworldPortalNarrative]] and [[FemaleSelfPossession]]: the emphasis is not merely on crossing into fantasy, but on a young protagonist learning how to act when familiar names, labor, bodies, and social rules change. That is why the source links it to Alice's active self-command.

[[94-jibuli-de-tiancaimen-wo-de-rensheng-jiaogei-reai-he-youyi-722350232]] adds the production and promotion layer. The episode says [[ToshioSuzuki|铃木敏夫]] helped steer the story toward No-Face rather than a more conventional escalating fantasy-battle route, then centered the publicity on No-Face after recognizing that the film's emotional structure was not only Chihiro versus the bathhouse world. The source also reads the film's success through post-bubble Japanese mood and psychological unease, making it a case for [[CreativeProducerMediation]] as well as fantasy authorship.

## Key Claims
- The film belongs to the same broad otherworld-entry pattern as Alice.
- Its relevance lies in child agency under strange rules, not only in visual fantasy.
- The comparison helps the episode connect Victorian children's literature to modern animation.
- The Ghibli source treats No-Face as a decisive story and marketing choice, not just an incidental character.
- Suzuki's intervention shows producer judgment shaping both the film's narrative emphasis and public entry point.
- The episode reads the film's success partly through changing era mood after Japan's bubble collapse.
