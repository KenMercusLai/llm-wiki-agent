# 黄庭坚 / Huang Tingjian

[[114-youdao-chi-ao-shangju-shi-liaoliao-pangxie-de-quwen-shihua-758424117]] presents 黄庭坚 as the clearest case of crab-eating moral ambivalence. The episode says he believed in Buddhism and had compassion-related hesitation, yet wrote many poems praising crab flavor and sometimes placed crab taste above more prestigious foods such as fish or lamb.

The source's point is not to condemn him as hypocritical, but to show [[EatingAndKillingMoralAmbivalence]]. Words such as "未免", "无罪", and "忍堪" become signs that literary appetite, Buddhist restraint, and ordinary pleasure can coexist uneasily in the same person. This makes Huang Tingjian a useful companion case to [[SuShi|苏轼 / 苏东坡]]: Song literati food writing can be joyous, witty, and ethically troubled at once.

## Key Claims
- Huang Tingjian's crab poems show taste as a literary and moral problem, not only a gastronomic preference.
- The source reads his hesitation as meaningful evidence of Buddhist compassion entering everyday eating.
- His case keeps [[CrabCulturalSymbolism]] from becoming a simple food-celebration story.
