# 螃蟹 / Crab

[[114-youdao-chi-ao-shangju-shi-liaoliao-pangxie-de-quwen-shihua-758424117]] makes the crab a dense cultural object rather than only a seasonal delicacy. The episode moves from autumn eating to old names such as 郭索, crab-catching tools, the "沪" place-name explanation, early crab sauce, giant-crab records, crab disasters in rice fields, dazha-crab naming debates, Song literati poems, [[HongLouMeng|《红楼梦》]] crab banquets, and Buddhist or folk stories where crabs become moral actors.

The earlier Mid-Autumn episode [[zhongqiu-kuaile-669967741]] had already introduced crab through [[ZhonghuaXieshi|《中华蟹史》]] as [[FestivalFoodMaterialHistory]]. Episode 114 deepens that branch into [[CrabCulturalSymbolism]]: the same animal can be food, pest, monster, tribute object, literary prop, proverb, political metaphor, and evidence of how humans project ethics onto nonhuman bodies.

## Key Claims
- Crabs enter Chinese cultural memory through eating practices, catching tools, place names, literature, folklore, and old natural-history imagination.
- The crab's meaning changes by context: an animal threatening rice fields is different from a late-autumn delicacy, a giant sea creature, or a poetic banquet object.
- Human stories about crabs often say more about people than about crabs, which connects the page to [[AnimalMoralProjection]].
- Crab eating can carry ethical friction when pleasure, killing, Buddhist compassion, and culinary praise sit together.
