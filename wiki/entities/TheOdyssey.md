# The Odyssey

The Odyssey is the Homeric epic discussed in [[in-it-to-bin-it-nigel-farage-v-count-binface-6a58abcdc152a357db17c20e]] through [[ChristopherNolan]]'s new film adaptation. The source presents the work as unusually difficult to adapt because it is structurally strange, culturally famous, and centered on [[Odysseus]], a hero whose ancient flaws do not map cleanly onto modern protagonist expectations.

[[96-hema-shishi-zai-jiazhuang-yongsheng-de-shidai-women-zhongdu-siwang-yiliyate-pian-725317514]] mentions The Odyssey as the companion Homeric epic whose relation to [[TheIliad|《伊利亚特》]] belongs to [[HomerQuestion|the Homer question]]. In that source, The Odyssey mainly serves as a boundary marker: the wooden horse, Greek homecomings, and later Trojan War aftermath sit outside the Iliad's compressed focus on [[Achilles|阿基里斯]]' rage.

[[ep269-cong-manwei-yuzhou-dao-aodesai-weihe-zongyao-fanpai-yingxiong-shishi-ltklotheqpup6oamyflfzpxtipsw]] makes the Odyssey more central. [[LiuChang|刘畅]] presents [[Odysseus|奥德修斯]]' route home as more than a chain of adventures: he wants to return to the [[AncientGreekHonorCommunity|community of recognition]] where household order, ritual, culture, and identity make sense.

The episode's larger claim is that every era remakes [[Homer]] in its own image. That does not make modernization automatically wrong, but it does make [[HomericAdaptationModernization]] a useful concept for separating productive reinterpretation from a smoothing away of moral strangeness.
