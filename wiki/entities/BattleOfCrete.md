# Battle of Crete

The Battle of Crete is the World War II campaign discussed in [[59-kelitedao-yangguang-haigui-shenhua-he-erzhan-zhanchang-646904469]]. The episode frames it as a strategically important fight for [[Crete]] after Germany's move into Greece, because the island's airfields could affect the eastern Mediterranean, Egypt, and oil-related routes.

In the source, the battle has three linked meanings. Militarily, it is presented as the first large-scale airborne operation and an early Allied use of Enigma-derived intelligence. Operationally, the episode stresses that intelligence can still fail when interpreted poorly or applied to the wrong target. Morally and locally, it emphasizes German occupation, civilian resistance, village-level memory, a tunnel museum near Chania, a priest's olive-tree intervention, and Maori soldiers covering Allied evacuation.

## Key Claims
- Airborne invasion made the battle a turning point in how the episode narrates modern warfare.
- Intelligence did not automatically produce victory; the episode says Allied interpretation and deployment choices still mattered.
- Local resistance and postwar memorial sites keep the battle from being only a military-operations story.
- The source connects the Crete case to ordinary people making risky choices under occupation.
